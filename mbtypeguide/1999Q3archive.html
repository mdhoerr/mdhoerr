Type and Family Dynamics 
by Mary Hoerr, September 15, 1999 

One reader asked me for resources on temperament (or type) as it relates systemically to family dynamics. I'm not sure I've found anything that directly answers this question, but here are a few places to start. 
The Association for Psychological Type (Net Links > nonprofits) has a Counseling and Psychotherapy Interest Area. Except for a few sample articles from the Bulletin of Psychological Type, there is little information available online. One of the articles, Eating Disorders: Beyond the Persona, discusses one case of type and family dynamics. 
Center for the Application of Psychologial Type (Net Links > nonprofits) provides research services for a fee. Their online listing of 350 bibliographic entries referencing the Journal of Psychological Type from the CAPT Bibliography included very few references to family topics. 
PersonalityType.com (Net Links > companies), run by the Tiegers, has a number of family related resources. Their book, Nurture by Nature (Net Links > books), applies knowledge of type to child development, and provides suggestions on how parents might interact with children of different types. Their experts page includes Joan Hubbard. They also have a bulletin board, that includes several threads related to family and relationship issues. 
Moms of Many Styles (Net Links > etc.) relates type to parenting style.

Using the MBTI to Write a Life Story as a Docudrama 
using Techniques from Writing Fiction 
A guest article provided for this site (see link review of Anne Hart's site in Net Links > teaching) 
by Anne Hart, M.A., (INFP) September 1, 1999 
Copyright by Anne Hart, M.A. 
The next time you're told to get a life, start a career writing about one. Become a biographer. 

Here's how to adapt a news clip biography to a long 1 1/2 to two-hour docudrama script about how to use MBTI® type in writing a person's life story. If you're familiar enough with personality type and the MBTI® instrument to write about it, then here are some fiction writing techniques to apply to a life story docudrama dramatizing the 16 MBTI® types in a "life story". 

Divide the story into three acts. Divide the characters into 16 types and/or four temperaments. 
Divide the paragraphs into Acts I, II, and III. Event and act now arrange the story, which was previously organized by chapter. 
Script 120pp 90pp 
Act I 1-24 
Act II 25- 45 
Act III 90- 75 
Identify MBTI (tm) type and how type influenced five major significant events also called turning points (in scriptwriting) and rites of passage (in anthropology) in a life story. These events would be crisis points where the character or you faces transitions, challenges and opportunities. 
A problem is solved, and the character changes, grows, learns from past mistakes, gains understanding and transformation, and finally achieves wisdom that is shared. The significant events would be universal. We all go through them, but they touch individuals in different ways at different times at nearly the same stages in life, although some are late or early bloomers. Color-code the paragraph at each turning point. 
In a 90-page script, the significant events will be scaled back thirty pages because the script ends sooner. In a 120-page screenplay, Act III begins on page 90. The resolution of the conflict and quick ending appears by page 120, leaving pages 90 to 120 to work on the outcome. 
Divide the story into six parts. 
Divide the story into six parts, or chapters, separated by the five major turning points in the story. 
Write the premise. 
Make the first chapter (which is an expanded hook) into the premise. The protagonist, the hero, heroine, or best character will be introduced by name and MBTI type in the first act. Then work in the protagonist's allies and mention their MBTI type and temperament. In the second scene of the first act, develop an important point about the antagonist and his allies revealing each person's type and how an important point related to type. 
How did type influence that important point? The person's decision? Choices? 
How did the character use his or her type to solve a problem? 
Pinpoint the significant events. 
From these main parts of the story, lay out the twelve most important events. These can be pinned up on a cork bulletin board or highlighted with a marker. At each turning point in the story, another crisis will happen that propels the action forward in another direction. 
Summarize each chapter. 
Write a paragraph summary of each chapter. The following examples are based on a 120-page screenplay. Use this format for your docudrama or news biography.  

Paragraph #1 (pp. 1-24) 
The premise tells the plot and introduces the hero. 
A critical situation is in motion. The hero and heroine are attracted to each other. Or some crisis occurs. Suspense begins to build. 
Conflict and physical attraction create crises at the cliffhanger on page 25-27 that spins the action forward in a new direction. 
Layer in the physical background. Draw storyboards to visualize the action scenes. Write the visuals first. The dialogue will come later. 
Paragraph # 2 (pp. 25-44) 
Continue the hook and develop the critical situation further. Develop the hero's own moral code based on the hero's type and temperament. Use strong suspense or strong sexual tension depending on genre - action or love story - or both. 
Bring in the subplot and the antagonist's strengths, but don't give too much information yet. Foreshadow the crisis to come by giving clues to the antagonist's allies. End with a cliffhanger to move the action further, faster, with each new crisis. 
Paragraph #3 (pp. 45-59) 
Enter the antagonist and all his allies. Tell how the antagonist comes into conflict with the protagonist for equal goals. What or who does the antagonist hate and why? What does the antagonist do? 
Introduce a whopper of a new event. It could be a big fight, adventure, or emotional love scene. Call into question whether the hero will be able to reach his goal due to the strong resistance, combat, or opposition from the antagonist and betrayal by his own allies. 
If the goal will ultimately be attained at the end, then make the goal more impossible to reach than ever. The hero withdraws from the heroine. Success withdraws at this point. 
Paragraph #4 (pp. 60-74) 
What does the protagonist do to take action against the antagonist? How does he or she do it? What adventure does the hero embark upon and what happens on this excursion or experience? The action can be either internal or external. What happens when the hero finally reaches the destination? What happens to the hero's friends or allies? How does the hero lead the allies? 
Page 60 is the midpoint in the script and where the background, suspense, and relationship conflict increase to the breaking point. This would be an opportunity to use a flashback or a time travel sequence in one surprise scene only. However, the majority of commercial linear scripts do not use flashback because it fails to move the action forward. Producers often reject scripts with flashback due to the increased production costs and the interruption in the plot that slows down the story. The trend is for tightly written shorter scripts where the suspense increases, as the countdown grows more intense as the deadline approaches. The hero's goal is to squeeze every challenge out of life in the time left before the explosive climax. The opposition is busy trying to diffuse the hero before disaster occurs. 
Paragraph #5 (pp. 75-89) 
Continue developing the tension and suspense - even with comedy - until the end of Act II. 
Paragraph #6 (pp. 90-120) 
There are now thirty pages left to finish the final act. What do you write in these final pages? The action and suspense must increase. Everything is now at its most critical boiling point of action. In the final act, which corresponds to the final chapter of the novel, the biggest crisis occurs. 
The biggest crisis has the strongest barriers and the most conflict between the hero and heroine, between the protagonist and the antagonist. Betrayals from the past come to fruition between allies. End the script with the most important action of the story. Build the adaptation around the twelve events, each with increasing drama, conflict, crises, tension, and 
suspense. Then bring the hero and his goal together at the end.

Typing Behavior, not People 
by Mary Hoerr, August 25, 1999 

I'm not good at "typing" other people. In fact, I won't say I know someone's type unless they've told me, and agreed that the type is accurate. 

So what good is type to me? How do I use it? 

Although I won't say someone is a certain type, I do regularly identify the function a person seems to be using at any given time. There are so many reasons a person may using a particular function, ranging from falsification of type to just plain common sense, that I find it very difficult to separate out the functions I usually see other people use from what their function preferences may be. And except for close friends and family, it doesn't really matter to me. 

If someone wants details, they are using their S function (regardless of what their preference may be), and I try to get down out of my N cloud and focus on details. If someone usually smiles at me and greets me in the morning, they are using the F function. So I shut down the analysis for the moment, and, amazingly enough, just smile and say "hi" back. 

In everyday interaction, I try to match the function coming at me. What an analytical way of saying, "just pay attention to people, and act appropriately." ; ) 
 

What do you think about typing behavior vs. people?

I can usually type a person after some observation.  I do think it is unfair to do it right off the bat, because the behavior at that moment may not be the person's characteristic way of acting.

INTJ 
For many years while growing up, I looked forward to the first day of school and said to myself,  "This is the year I'll make friends. This is the year there'll be friendly people in my class. I'm a year older now, and maybe this year I'll better know how to make friends. If not this year, then next year, maybe I'm just not old enough and haven't developed the ability yet. But hey, I'm smart and if I can handle schoolwork and go in there with a positive attitude towards making friends, one of these years it's going to happen for me."  
Of course, it never happened, due to the INTJ intensity of my introversion, my incredible lack of even pretending to fit into peer groups, my preferring to contemplate the possibilities of science fiction rather than being interested in who was seen with whom at the mall and what clothes they bought there, and my making sure I had my homework done to the best of my ability each day. And of course it never happened, due to my always waiting to be approached by people whom I thought would accept me for myself even though I broke all of their social rituals, and not knowing how to approach them nor even how best to respond to them if by some miracle they would have approached me. 
Now that I've discovered Myers-Briggs, it's helped me open a window that was formrely always slammed shut in my face with the blinds drawn. Now that I have a chance to observe people and figure out what personality type they belong to, I have a chance to relate to them on their terms. So I am all in favor of typing people. Of course I know that each person is an individual who can't be expected to conform strictly to standardized tests seeking to describe the mythical  "average" person. But if I were forced to go back to trying to figure out people based on their behavior of the moment at a given time, without being able to place it in the context of their normal "modus operandi" or preferred method of functioning, I'd find myself once again placed back into a sea of chaos.

We observe a common, even stereotypical, set of attributes and say, "Ah, (he or she) is American." Or French. Or Japanese. 
Yet beneath these broad categories we discover more divisions. What state is this American from (What Department is this French person from?) What city? Is this person urban or rural? Educated or illiterate? Young or old? Artistic or practical? Therefore, while it is enjoyable and even useful to class people by personality type, any deeper look opens even more questions and doors. Fundamentally we are individual.

Not by Type Alone 
by Mary Hoerr, August 9, 1999 

While surfing the web looking for links and information on type, I came across someone who described how his boss used type. The boss would give him something to do, and say he would like it because "his type likes that kind of thing." When the employee said he didn't really, the boss would say the employee just didn't really understand type, and insisted he would like the task. The boss and employee were of different types. 

I could think of three reasons, just to start out, why the boss could have been wrong about the task. First of all, maybe the boss didn't know enough about his employee's type to know what that type preferred. He was getting feedback from the horse's mouth, and not listening. 

Secondly, maybe the employee had been mistyped - several sources I've read place the percentage of people that are assigned the wrong type by the MBTI® instrument at 25%. And the percentage is probably even higher among those who just take an MB-based indicator (like the Keirsey sorter) without any professional interpretation or counselling on the results. 

Finally, maybe some factor unrelated to type is at work here. For example, just because an ENTP tends to be a nonconformist who questions authority, it does not mean that this particular ENTP won't be a strictly practicing member of her religious group - whether a Roman Catholic who fasts on Ash Wednesday or an Orthodox Jew who won't eat pork. 

The important thing to remember is that personality type is only one factor, and in a particular case, it may not be the most important one. It doesn't substitute for listening to the individual. 
 
Have you ever had someone pay more attention to what they thought about your type than to what you had actually said? Or have you seen this happen to someone else? 

I got three responses to this question. An INTP and an INFJ just provided their type, with no other response. One INTJ wrote the following: 

Yes, particularly by "professionals" who do not understand that MB gives a general profile and/or indicators. That MB type is, in my opinion, a tool to help understand another person but not to be used as the only indicator of behavior and/or personality.

On Being S-deficient 
by Mary Hoerr, July 26, 1999

Several months ago, a co-worker turned 40. Her office was decorated with black balloons, and there were treats in the kitchen. In order to get to my desk, I passed both the kitchen, where I noted the treats, and her office, where I did not note the balloons. I sat down at my desk, and checked my email, which is always the first thing I do when I get to work. There, I read the email notice that she had turned 40, and the treats were in honor of her birthday. I went to her office, wished her happy birthday, and told her I hadn't even noticed the balloons. How Knowing Type Helps Me 
by Mary Hoerr, July 19, 1999 

In surfing the web, I came across someone who asked what real difference knowing about type made to a person. One person emailed me that knowing about type would have helped him in school, if he had known about it sooner. Others think it helps them understand themselves. 

It got me to thinking how it has made a difference to me. 

First of all, it helped me to make sense of the different ways people act, and why I sometimes unintentionally insulted people (and sometimes, unintentionally got a laugh from some wry comment). Now that I know about the S vs N way of seeing the world, I can try to "shift" to an S perspective, instead of just wondering why I keep missing details. 

Those are two major differences knowing type has made to me. How has knowing about type made a difference to you?

What Test (Indicator) Should I Take? 
by Mary Hoerr, July 12, 1999, updated 10 May 2000

From time to time, I get email asking me about Myers-Briggs™ testing. Before I continue, I must make two important points.
First of all, any questionnaire, sorter, or indicator based on Myers-Briggs™ theory should not be considered a "test." This is an important point, because I don't trust any MB-based instrument that doesn't make this clear. Most sites have to put the word "test" on their pages somewhere because people new to MB always search on "test" - which is why this article uses "test." But somewhere, prominantly the site should always tell you this is not a test and there are no right or wrong answers, just like there's no such thing as a right or wrong hair color or town of birth.
Secondly, you must know that I am not certified to administer the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator ® (MBTI®) instrument. My knowledge comes from reading and having taken the MBTI® instrument at a workshop. The theory behind the MBTI® instrument goes back to Jung. If you want to get technical, check out C. G. Jung, Analytical Psychology, and Culture,  the Association for Psychological Type or the Center for Applications of Psychological Type, Inc. 
The most certain way to determine your type is to take the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator® (MBTI®) instrument from someone who is licensed to administer it. The fee should always include a certain amount of consulting and interpretation with you after you take the indicator. You normally have to be certified to administer the indicator before you can get a copy of it. For information on getting certified, go to right hand column of Net Links > tests.
I list two [as of 10 May 2000, there were five] companies that administer the MBTI® instrument online at the top of Net Links > tests. Since prices may change, you will need to go to the site of each organization listed to get the latest price. Prices of anywhere from $50 to $150 would not be unreasonable, since they include at least one hour (and sometimes more) of professional counselling on the results of the test. 
The other questionnaires listed on my tests page are based on Myers-Briggs™ theory, or Keirsey's version of the Myers-Briggs™ theory. They can be taken on-line for no fee or a very small fee, but they are not as reliable, and do not include any professional interpretation and counselling on the results. Finally, the Benziger Thinking Styles Assessment (BTSA) also builds on Myers-Briggs™ theory, and includes additional analysis to determine if you may be falsifying your type. 
If you want to get reasonable certainty that you know your type, expect to pay at least $30, probably more, for the MBTI® instrument or the BTSA to be professionally administered. If you're just interested in type, you may want to start out by reading a couple of books on type, and take several of the free on-line sorters to get started. My favorite free online sorter right now is the MMDI available online at the Team Technology site. The most popular sorter is probably the Keirsey Temperament Sorter.

eBTSA Available

by Dr. Katherine Benziger, July 5, 1999 
copyright 1999 by Dr. Katherine Benziger

Thank you for your interest in the electronic Benziger Thinking Styles Assessment (eBTSA). The BTSA is a powerful tool which many have found to offer helpful and healing information. 

All those completing the eBTSA receive a 24 to 28 page personal FEEDBACK providing them with an analysis of: 

  1. their natural thinking style, 
  2. their tendency to FALSIFY TYPE, 
  3. the ways if any in which Falsifying Type has affected them, and 
  4. a detailed listing of activities and skills to develop and use which use their natural lead function as well as a listing of those which do not and as such tend to be more exhausting. 

In other words, each BENZIGER BREAKTHROUGH FEEDBACK provides you with detailed GUIDELINES for using your results to improve:  your quality of life, personal sense of meaninfulness, job satisfaction and health.

To take the eBTSA and obtain your personal breakthrough feedback: 

  1. Fax the following order and payment instruction to Dr. Benziger in the USA at: 970-468-1806: Your name as it appears on the card; Your card number; The Expiration Date on the Card; The Amount you are asking us to charge; Permission to charge that amount to your card.
  2. GO TO http://www.benziger.org/ebtsa to download the eBTSA software to a PC (sorry, no Mac version available). Then, install the software using the password  THRIVING   in capital letters.
  3. COMPLETE the eBTSA yourself, making sure when you have done so that you CREATE A DATA FILE when it asks you if you want to do so. 
  4. Then, RETURN your electronic data file to Dr. Benziger, by sending her your data file as an attachment to an e-mail. Dr. Benziger's e-mail address is katherine.benziger@airmail.net. Electronic assessments take 1 to 3 days to process. 
  5. When your BREAKTHROUGH FEEDBACK is READY, Dr. Benziger will send it you your FEEDBACK as an attachment to an e-mail along with instructions for PRINTING it.
  6. Upon RECEIVING your personal FEEDBACK you will want to READ it carefully. Having a copy of The Art of Using Your Whole Brain and or Dr. Benziger's Audio Tapes on hand is helpful. Most people find their FEEDBACK to be empowering and healing in a variety of ways. 


    To SUPPLEMENT what they have learned from their FEEDBACK, some people like to schedule a conference call with Dr. Benziger. Should you wish to talk with Dr. Benziger by phone, about your results, she will be glad to go over your FEEDBACK or about questions which reading your FEEDBACK raised in your mind, contact Dr. Benziger by e-mail at     katherine.benziger@airmail.net. Such conference calls are made by appointment and generally last about an hour. Dr. Benziger's hourly rate is $75.

Is your type different at work?
