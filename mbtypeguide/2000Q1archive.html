S Content and N Context 
by Mary Hoerr, March 29, 2000 

I have found the distinction between S and N being described as being like that between content and context, or like that between data and pattern. Here is how I understand that statement: S wants to make the meaning most real; N wants to make the reality most meaningful.
Everything that exists has both pattern and content. For example, a tree has both the data that makes it real (size, color, bark, roots) and it is also a pattern. The meaning/realtiy Tree consists of both the pattern "tree" and the dataset [tree]. It is possible to talk as if the two could be separated, but I cannot conceive of any case where this actually occurs.
For S, patterns are used to give meaning to the collection of data (Si), or to provide a purpose for the transformation of data (Se). Si enjoys collection of data, Se enjoys manipulation of data as a realization of meaning. The meaning or form inherent in the data can be realized by collecting more and more relevant data, like a high resolution picture (Si), or by transforming the data to conform to its inherent form or meaning. as when Michaelangelo "released" the statue of David that he "saw" in the block of marble (Se).
For N, data are used to anchor the application of patterns (Ni) or to provide impetus for the creation / recognition of new patterns (Ne). Ni enjoys accumulating patterns to give meaning to the data, Ne enjoys imagining new patterns to give meaning to the data. The reality can be made meaningful either by superimposing the greatest number of patterns on it, such as when [tree] is seen as a plaything for children, a producer of oxygen and an object for poetry (Ni), or by creating a new pattern that conforms most perfectly to its reality, as when someone came up with category/pattern of "mammals" to describe the common data shared by a wide range of data (Ne).

Teams, Temperament and Type 
by Mary Hoerr, March 22, 2000 

Michael Finley's article, in which he named "The Myth of Personality Type" as one of the Four Myths about Teams [Link review: This Week] got me to thinking about the use of type in teams. He is not convinced that [MBTI® instrument] "personality reliably and consistently reveals itself in outside behaviors ... There are too many confounding life experiences which modify what we are into how we behave." Before I continue, I would like to remind my readers that while I consider myself reasonably well-read on the subject of Myers-Briggs™ type, I am not certified to administer the MBTI® instrument.
In my reading, I have come across two factors that make me think that people are less likely to arrive at their "true type" when they take the MBTI® instrument in a business setting. One factor is that people will often answer the MBTI® instrument according to how they act at work, which may not reflect their true type, or according to how they think their company wants them to answer. The second is that it can take considerable time and reflection before a person finally ends up with their true type, time which may not be available in a business setting.
If this is true for determining one's own type, the factors may be magnified when it comes to trying to determine the type of others. Type practitioners themselves develop an awareness of type pattern that only comes after long practice and experience, and even then are careful not to impose their own ideas of a person's type onto that person.
What does this mean for using type in a business setting? Perhaps rather than typing the team members, it would be more useful to use functions and temperament. A knowledge of temperament can help leaders understand better how to motivate and reward team members. It is much easier to determine, through trial and error if nothing else, what a team member's temperament might be. Most important is realizing how the same reward can be perceived very differently by different people. For example, the NT "reward" of a new and interesting problem to solve can seem like a punishment to another temperament.
Knowledge of functions could be used in two areas: Firstly, as a means of identifying and adapting to different communication style. Secondly, it can be used as a means of identifying blind spots.
In the first case, it is not necessary to make any assumptions about another person's type. A person can and often does use different function "dialects" for a number of reasons. All that is necessary is to decide how they are communicating, and try to match that style. Dick Thompson talks about The Communication Wheel (tm) [My link review at Net Links > companies High Performing Systems]. Personally, I don't get into the detail of the different "dialects" according to which function is dominant and which is auxiliary. It is enough for my use to identify explicitly to myself when someone is speaking factually, and make myself focus on the facts (S-talk) or abstractly, in which case I would focus on the big picture (N-talk), with similar adjustments for other functions.
In the second case, identifying blind spots, it is also not strictly necessary to identify the types of the team members. Isabel Myers has formulated a model of decision making that incorporates all functions in making a decision. First use S to determine the facts, then N to brainstorm possibilities, T to analyze cause and effect, and finally F to determine how the solutions fit in with corporate values and how they will affect people. It may well be that in the process of going through this process, a team may discover one or more area that causes difficulty. This would be an indication that the team has discovered a blind spot.
I believe a knowledge of individual team member's type could also help considerably in these areas, but since it may be difficult to determine this in the course of a business team building exercise (or even in the course of several years), it may be more practical to focus on the important contributions of each function preference, and on the different motivations implied by the four temperaments.

Experiences of Type 
by S Myers, March 15, 2000 
copyright by S Myers 2000  
Steve Myers is from Team Technology, which is a "training consultancy that specialises in team building, leadership development and interpersonal skills training". Go to my link review.

In a galaxy far, far away, and in a land that no one yet knows, there was a wise Teacher and a young Jedi knight.
"What must I learn today?" said the knight.
"You must learn the ancient art of Type" said the Teacher. "Go to the Land of Individuation, and find yourself eight different Types, so that you can find yourself. Experience Type. Experience yourself."
The Jedi knight travelled far and entered the Land of Individuation, whereupon he met a Ti.
"I want to experience type" said the knight.
"Ah!" said the Ti. "Your desire to experience type arises from your need to individuate."
"I don't want to know what causes my desire" replied the knight, "I want to experience Type".
"Ah!" said the Ti. "Your lack of interest in causality is a reflection of your Type."
"I don't want to see a reflection" said the knight, "I want to experience Type."
"But I have explained it to you - so you *have* experienced it."
The knight, being a little unsure about this, continued the journey and met two people engaged in an argument - Fi and Te.
"Excuse me" said the knight, "but can either of you help me to experience Type?"
"Sure" said the Te, "What you need to do is..."
"Don't tell the knight what to do!" interrupted the Fi, "this is an individual who needs to be understood."
"You are confusing the issue" said Te. "The knight asked for objective guidance, so I'm going to give it."
"No!" said Fi. "The knight is on a quest to search for identity."
"I don't want guidance or identity" said the Jedi knight, "I want to experience Type." And the Jedi knight moved on.
Suddenly, a magician with a long white beard appeared in the middle of the road.
"Where did you come from?" asked the Jedi knight.
"I do not know" replied the magician. "But I am here, and I am Ni, of that I am certain."
"He comes from the place that cannot be seen" boomed a voice from behind.
The Jedi knight turned to see a wise sage walking up the road.
"I *know* where I have come from" said the sage. "I am Si, and I am also certain."
"Can either of you help me to experience Type?" asked the Jedi knight.
"Type" said Ni, "is a wonderfully mystical concept that transcends the cosmos..." and in a flash, Ni leapt across the galaxy and could be seen twinkling in a distant star.
"Tell me about yourself" Si asked the Jedi knight, "What do you know of type? Where are you from? Where are you going? Where have you been?"
"STOP!" said the knight, "I don't want to be interrogated. I just want to experience Type.
Suddenly, they were interrupted by an athlete running along the road, sporting a T-shirt with the symbols 'Se' blazoned across the front. Se ran right into the knight, who was knocked to the floor.
"Sorry, can't stop" shouted Se. "Just gotta do something..."
The Jedi knight watched Se racing into the distance and disappearing over the brow of the road.
When the Jedi Knight turned back, Si had departed, having dropped a book.
"What do I do with this?" said the Jedi knight.
"You could use it to catch mice" said a voice.
"Where are you?" asked the Jedi knight, because he couldn't see the person who had spoken.
"I am Ne" came the reply, "and I am just around the corner."
"Well, come out" said the knight, "so that I can see you.
"I can't come out" said Ne "otherwise I would lose interest. I am only interested in what lies around the corner. If I go around the corner, then what lies there has no interest for me."
The Jedi knight's face fell glum at this. "Am I never going to experience Type?"
"Don't look so unhappy" said another voice, and the Jedi turned to see a warm, caring face emerging from the roadside bushes. "I am Fe, and I'll take care of you."
"I don't want anyone to take care of me" cried the Jedi. "I just want to experience Type."
Then the Jedi looked up to the sky and, through meditation, transported back to the faraway land to see the Teacher.
"Have you experienced Type?" said the Teacher.
"Alas, no" said the Jedi knight. "I've been analysed, bossed around, told I need an identity, subjected to mystical ramblings, interrogated, bustled out of the way, told that I'm only interesting if I can't be seen, and molly-coddled. But I've not experienced Type."
"On the contrary!" said the Teacher. "Your experience is complete!"

MB Type and Gender 
by Mary D. Hoerr, March 8, 2000 

Are there differences in type preferences between men and women? The only difference appears to be that 2/3 of women may prefer F, while 2/3 of men may prefer T. There are some interesting descriptions of gender differences in dealing with types in Otto Kroeger's Type Talk book. See my Otto Kroeger Associates link review. 
For example, a woman with an ISFJ profile may have almost "too few" gender issues, since all of her preferences fall nicely in line with traditional definitions of femininity. However, a man with the same profile may definitely have conflicts to work through. 
I think because of socialization of girls in United States society, it is actually better to be a female INTP than a male one. Extraverted Feeling is definitely our weak point, and the males get by without developing it much longer - which I think hurts their relationships. 
I think I ought to say, however, that I am not aware of any definitive study showing even the T/F differences between men and women. Niednagel seems to think that the only reason there seem to be more F women and more T men is because they are more in line with traditional gender roles. He has a book called "Your Key to Sports Success", which relates type and sports. See my book review. See my Brain Type Institute link review.

Jung's Hierarchy of Mental Functions 
by C. Ross Reinhold, M.S., March 1, 2000 
copyright by C. Ross Reinhold 2000  
Ross is an experienced and qualified administrator of all three levels of the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator® instrument and a skilled practitioner in its applications to business settings. Currently, he serves as webmaster and publisher of Personality Pathways, (Link reviews > nonprofits) an internet resource for human development practitioners interested in applications of the MBTI® instrument and psychological type to personal and organizational development. They are currently asking for presentations for "Unlocking the Mysteries of Personality" the Year 2000 Great Lakes APT conference.

Type literature seems to be of two minds on the developmental hierarchy for Jung's mental functions. One of the causes of this discrepancy is that Jung went on to explore entirely new themes and interests sometime after writing Psychological Types. In some respect, his theory was an unfinished symphony. Isabel Myers was one of the more prominent students of Jung's writing who attempted to finish the piece. 
People like Myers and contemporary Jungian theorist John Beebe posit a tapestry of dynamic balance among the judging and perceiving functions in the introverted and extraverted attitudes. Thus the dominant and auxiliary functions are in opposite attitudes as are the third (tertiary) and fourth (inferior) mental functions. The judging and perceiving functions are also aligned in opposite attitudes. So if the dominant is judging, then the auxiliary is perceiving, and then the tertiary (third) is the other judging function. Likewise the fourth function in the hierarchy is the other perceiving function. I visualize this as a zigzag pattern in a two column, four celled table with extraversion and introversion the labels at the top of the column. 
Beebe actually extends this four level hierarchy to eight by differentiating the mental functions by their attitude. So there are two forms of Intuition (extraverted and introverted), two forms of Feeling (extraverted and introverted), and so on. The lower case letters attached to the function symbol indicates these distinctions. Ne stands of extraverted Intuition, Ni stands for its introverted form. The eight mental function hierarchy continues the same pattern of zigzag, in/out juxtaposition of opposites laid out for the first four mental functions. People following this particular model of type development find it both a reasonable extension of Jung's model and helpful to identify with more precision what might be the developmental challenges encountered as adults mature. In this model the most inferior mental function is that which is both the opposite pole of the S/N or T/F dichotomy and in the same attitude. Thus the true inferior or opposite of dominant introverted Intuition is introverted Sensing. 
Other Jungian scholars and students sometimes offer a contrary viewpoint to the above model. They have found passages in Psychological Types suggesting that Jung believed that the auxiliary, tertiary, and inferior functions all resided in the attitude opposite that of the dominant. Those who disagree with this interpretation acknowledge that indeed Jung made such statements, but they argue, this is but one of many seemingly contradictory elements in Jung's piece. And since he left this territory to explore other worlds, we don't have volumes of subsequent writings to examine for further clarity on this point. 
Perhaps Jung formulated this idea of the dominant being alone in its' attitude through observation of cases of apparent development of extraverted forms of the auxiliary, tertiary, and inferior functions. Rather than attribute this to the effect of environment or nurture, he may have modified his evolving model to posit a "nature" explanation. 
As a model I prefer the symmetry of the dynamically balanced in/out, yin/yang pattern found in Myers' and Beebe's writings. I think this harmonizes well with the primary themes of Jung's work. It sits on my mind in a more comprehendible, orderly and comfortable way than the other one. Furthermore, the idea of keeping the dominant alone in its attitude presents additional challenges when attempting to extend the model to the eight mental function pattern that is being used in most discussions of type dynamics. It would steep us in further complexity and thus become less useful.

Prayer and Temperament: Different Prayer Forms for Different Personality Types Book Review

by Mary Hoerr, February 23, 2000  
Prayer and Temperament: Different Prayer Forms for Different Personality Type, by Chester P. Michael and Marie C. Norrisey, is the result of the 1982 Prayer and Temperament research project involving 457 people. The authors identify several prayer styles that have traditionally been used in Catholic prayer, and relate them to different temperaments. They then give suggestions for different temperaments and types. 
The idea is to find the form of prayer that comes easiest to your type or temperament, and make that your usual prayer type. However, the authors recommend becoming acquainted with all the prayer forms - they believe that the prayer form that is most difficult for you, if practiced when you have the time and energy to devote to it, may actually provide some of your richest prayer experiences, since it draws on your shadow function. However, it would be too difficult to use your most difficult prayer form as your most common form - they believe you would get discouraged and neglect your prayer life. 
Because in recent centuries, Catholic seminaries have stressed the NT or Thomistic prayer form for priests, brothers, and sisters, there is something of a reaction against this prayer form. It focuses on a logical and intellectual understanding of scripture and religious themes, leading to a better understanding of God as Truth. This would be my favored prayer form. Although it may be stressed in seminaries, I have virtually never run across this form among the laity. In fact, my most common experience is to be told I'm not really praying - just studying. 
The SJ or Ignatian prayer form involves trying to recreate in vivid detail a scene from the Bible or other useful religious text, and place yourself in that scene. This type of prayer attempts to make real the SJ's perception of God in the history of salvation. According to the book, this is my most difficult prayer form, which I should practice perhaps once a week. It is most likely to provide my most transcendent experiences. I've never actually tried it. Just reading the description made me tired. 
The SP or Franciscan prayer form tends to concentrate on the prayer of good works, and openness to the presence of the Holy Spirit. Interestingly, the authors suggest that both St. Francis of Assisi, and St. Peter the Apostle were ESFPs. This concentrates on God as Beauty or Joy. 
The NF or Augustinian prayer form uses bible or religious text to find a personal meaning for the texts. This form concentrates on God as Love. The authors believe that most canonized saints (just means those the Catholic Church is convinced are saints) are of this temperament. 
This is an incredibly detailed book, providing prayer suggestions not only by temperament but by individual type. They relate the historic use of prayer forms, as well as how the different temperaments relate to various parts of the Mass and other Catholic religious practices. There's a lot more there than my short descriptions show.

Inside my Head: INTP and INFP 
by Mary Hoerr, March 8, 1999 (reprinted February 16, 2000 )

In my article The Search for Perfection a couple of weeks ago, I described what I imagined the inner world of the SJ, especially those with introverted Sensing dominant (ISFJ and ISTJ) looked like. This week I'm going to try the same thing on the two intuitive types who have a dominant introverted judging function: INTP and INFP. 
Because I am introvert, I "live" in my inner world, not in the external world. This should also apply to the INFP. 
Because my dominant function is a judging function, I organize where I live - that is, my inner world. This should also apply to the INFP. 
Because my judging function is T, I organize according to objective truth, which I subjectively believe in. The INFP would organize according to value judgments and feeling. 
My auxiliary function is iNtuition, which is extroverted. External information acts as a trigger to my inner organizing. I don't notice my surroundings at all, except as a point of departure for current interests. In fact, it sometimes seems that the greatest indicator of my iNtuitive preference is my lack of Sensing observation. I miss my exit, I don't notice the large birthday balloons festooning a coworker's office, and so on. Most of the time, when I am not forced to interact with the outside world, I am organizing my thoughts on some subject. One current interest is an attempt to depict to myself the inner and outer worlds of each temperament and type. I imagine that an INFP would be organizing how she values and feels about things. 
I picture the inner world of my mind as a relational database. Data is collected, but only retained when it can be logically connected with pieces of data. One connection only - name with person, for example - is not good for remembering. Multiple connections are better. 
I picture the inner world of an INFP mind as a spider web. As in my case, there are many connections, going in many directions. But the connections are not defined logically, but by "feel", just as one can imagine "feeling" and "pulling on" spider web strands of different lengths and thicknesses. 
In both cases, the N preference implies multiple connections among many pieces of information. The dominant T preference implies logically defined connections, manipulated by changing formulae. The dominant F preference implies elastic, "felt" connections which are "moved" and "pulled" to change them. Somehow the NT structure seems "square" to me and the NF structure seems "oval".

Traveling Type
by Mary Hoerr, February 9, 2000  
Since finding the Travel Types site, I got to thinking about my own travel experience, and how it relates to my type. My all time favorite vacation was when I spent a few weeks in Germany with a Eurail pass and youth hostel information. I was by myself, and had no schedule for the entire time. I had two goals - I wanted to see an outdoor rock concert in Germany, and I wanted to visit the hometown of a German comedian called Otto - a small town in the north of Germany in a province called Eastern Friesland. I achieved both my goals.
My normal routine during the weeks on my own was to get to a Youth Hostel by mid afternoon and check in. Then I would wander around wherever I was. At supper at the hostel, or afterwards in the common room, I would hook up with whoever happened to be there, and do whatever they were doing that evening, or the next morning. I ended up visiting a castle in one town, and a famous church organ in Ulm that way. Then, I would go to the train station, see what trains were leaving in the next half hour or so, and go wherever seemed interesting.
This almost seems like a charicature of my type - alone most of the time, no schedule, and no obligations to any other person. And I got to improve my German (NT skills improvement).

What Type Am I? Book Review 
by Mary Hoerr, February 2, 2000  
What Type Am I?, by Renee Baron, is a good introduction to type. First, the functions are described, and you answer several of the usual kinds of questions for each function pair to get a preliminary idea of your type. The chapter for each function pair also includes a section on how to get along with persons with a particular preference, and suggestions for people who have a particular preference: ie, "How to get along with Sensors" and "Suggestions for iNtuitives". After the functions, there are sixteen short type descriptions.< 
The next chapters introduce the four temperaments. She calls the SJs Duty Seekers, the SPs Action Seekers, The NTs Knowledge Seekers, and the NFs Ideal Seekers. Each temperament chapter also includes a detailed section for each of the four types that share that temperament. The section covers work, relationships, leisure, and suggestions. 
Perhaps my favorite part of the book were the numerous cartoons and quotations used to illustrate each concept. Here are a few examples:

Minding your Js and Ps
by Mary Hoerr, January 26, 2000  
Occasionally, I read about people who are confused about whether they are J or P. Although a common indication of J is neatness and to-do lists, while Ps tend to look less organized, that's not what J and P are really about. The J or P identifies which function tends to be used in the outside world. 
Someone who is J prefers to use her judging function, that is, the T or F function, in the outside world. Since the judging function is what makes order of our perceptions, that means you look organized to the outside world. 
But everyone has a preferred judging function, either as their dominant or axiliary function. So the P preference just means your ordering, judging function is turned inward, rather outward where others can see it. 
If you are also an extrovert, you not only show your judging to the outside world - this function also happens to be your dominant function. Some people think that you develop your second function more as you get older. So if you are an extrovert, and you think your type is "changing" from J to P or vice versa, it may just be that you are developing your second function more, which is focussed in the other direction. An extraverted J may seem more open as he develops his auxiliary N or S function; an extraverted P may look to be putting more order to her life as she develops her auxiliary T or F. 
An intravert is in an interesting situation. Her dominant function is focussed inward. An intraverted P seems less organized to the world, because she shows her perceiving function to the outside. However, inwardly she prefers a judging function, and thus may experience herself as extremely organized.

My Pet
by Mary Hoerr, January 19, 2000  
Inspired by Dr. Lachman's book, "Dogs on the Couch", I trying to recall my relationship with pets. I've only had one - when I was growing up. He was a toy French poodle I named Jaques, because that was the only French I knew at the time, from "Frere Jacques". Dr. Lachman suggests that poodles might be suited to the hardworking SJ type. I remember him jumping and barking whenever anyone came to the door. He never bit, and I don't remember him ever growling until I came back from college years ago to find him old and arthritic.  
Although he was "my" dog, my mom basically housebroke him and all I had to do was remember to feed him once a day. Occassionally, I would have to give him a bath, but Mom must have filled in a lot, because I didn't bathe him on anything near a regular basis. We used to say he was perfectly safe around children, and the younger the child, the gentler he was. He was never taken for walks (that I remember) by anyone, and certainly not by me. He was let loose in our large edge-of-a-small-town yard pretty much whenever he wanted, and never seemed to go further than a yard or two in any direction. 
My clearest memories of him are holding him on my lap and petting him while watching TV. The perfect low-maintenance, convenient, comforting relationship. 
My type is INTP - Dr. Lachman suggests that NT types might get along well with dogs that need firmer boundary setting. However, setting boundaries for dogs sounds like way too much work for me. What I need is a low energy, low dominance dog that I can pet from time to time without spending too much time taking care of it. Actually, sounds like any dog would do, as long as someone else did the work.

Winning Resumes for Computer Personnel Book Review 
by Mary Hoerr, January 12, 2000  
We all know that the most common preferences for Information Technology workers are I and T, right? Well, Winning Resumes for Computer Personnel by Anne Hart reminds us that there is more to computer work than code and machines. She opens up the field to a full range of careers including coding and hardware, of course, but also graphic design, human resources, technical writing, and more. 
She uses type to help you determine what kinds of jobs you might prefer, and what your strong points are likely to be. Her description of each preference uses example related to the job search, or the job environment. Thus, in her description of extroversion, she recommends considering a job where there is "extensive contact with clients because people contact energizes you and being alone drains you." Someone with the S preference will probably "want to see ideas applied to real solutions" while if you prefer N, you can probably "find a thousand ways to market a product or service, or a hundred businesses that utilize one skill." 
If the descriptions of each preference, and the short profiles of each type aren't enough to help you determine your type, there is also a Computer Career Preference Classifier, with 74 multiple choice questions. Based on Myers-Briggs™ concepts (but not the official MBTI® instrument), this classifier differs from the MBTI® instrument and other Myers-Briggs™ based sorters because every question relates directly to a job or computer industry related situation. For example, if you had a choice between two workshops offered by your employer, would you rather attend the one on how to repair your personal computer, or the one on how to write novels about the future of computers? 
Of course, there are also pages and pages of sample resumes, as well as a chapter analyzing two resumes to determine how to make them more effective.
