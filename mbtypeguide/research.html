Relating Type and Neurology (added 28 October 2000)
Loren Friesen has published to the web his book A Programmer’s Guide to the Mind, which associates various types of brain functioning to different parts of the brain. He comes up with some of the same divisions as Myers-Briggs™ theory. However, where Myers-Briggs™ theory sees dichotomies, Friesen sees modes. In a chapter of his book, he explains that he sees Myers-Briggs™ theory as a subset that can be included in his theory. This is truly an entire book on the web, and I didn't get through it all. There is the usual problem I see that connects T with objective and F with subjective. I prefer Lenore Thompson's description that calls T impersonal and F personal, but each come in both objective and subjective forms (see my article: Feeling - Inside and Out ).

More Theory on how F Develops (added 28 October 2000)
In January 1999, I referenced John Fudjack & Patricia Dinkelaker research on the development of the F function (Net Links > research), which they divided into five levels. This year, they have posted articles on three of the five levels of F function development as part of 'An In-Depth Look at 'Theories of Emotion'.

Handwriting Analysis and Research Project (added 26 July 2000)
Chicken Scratch Handwriting Analysis provides resources for people interested in handwriting analysis. Currently, the site includes an article that attempts to relate handwriting to Myers-Briggs™ functions. She relates printscript to both the N and the P preferences, which sure fits me. I couldn't follow a lot of the other handwriting characteristics, so I'm not sure if the I and T descriptions fit me too. In addition, Kristen is conducting research on the relationship between type and handwriting. If you decide to participate, you'll get a free handwriting analysis.

Programmer's Personality Type (added 19 Jul 2000)
In his article Orphans Preferred, by Steve McConnell describes various traits of software programmers, including personality type. The single most common type was ISTJ, but he also finds that good software designers tend to be good at moving between S-N, T-F and P-J.

Technology Education / Industrial Arts Teachers (added 5 July 2000)
In their article The Relationship Between Psychological Type and Professional Orientation Among Technology Education Teachers, Robert C. Wicklein and Jay W. Rojewski provide the results of their research into the predominant types of technology education teachers. The most common types (accounting for 69% of the respondants) were ESTJ, ISTJ, ENTJ, and ENFJ. The Keirsey Sorter was used to determine type.

Eating Disorders Forums Check out Type (added 28 June 2000)
One of the bulletin boards on the Something Fishy Eating Disorder site has had a few topics on Myers-Briggs™ types. Members have been taking the Keirsey Sorter online to see if certain eating disorders are more common for certain types. The Anorexia board seems to have started it in October 1999 with Anyone take the Meyers-Briggs personality test? Out of 39 posts to the Anorexia board, INFJ's again predominated at 19, with the next largest number of posts for ISFJ with 4. Then the Bulimia board took it up in November 1999 with Myers-Briggs™ personality test. Out of 17 posts far away the largest number were INFJ at 7. The next largest number a three-way tie between INTP, INTJ and ENTJ with only 2 each. Then the Anorexia and Bulimia board took it up again just recently in May 2000 with Personality Test. Out of 23 people who posted their types, INFJ was most common at 7, with the next most common being INTJ at 3. You may also want to check out my link review of a professional's article on eating disorders and type at Net Links > etc. from September 1999.

Type Factors in Psi (added 3 May 2000)
Does Psi Exist? Replicable Evidence for an Anomalous Process of Information Transfer, by Daryl J. Bem and Charles Honorton, describes the problems with designing a study to show whether or not there is such a thing as psi, also called ESP. Among several things positively correlated with an apparent occurance of psi are E, F and P. Interestingly, there was no correlation between psi and a preference for N. Extroverted perception and introverted feeling judgement were correlated. This is counter to many type descriptions, that associate psychic capability (if it exists) with NF's and especially INFJ or INFP.

Who Wants to Retire Early? (added 3 May 2000)
John P. Greaney, editor of the Retire Early Home Page, puts together quite a bit of research in an attempt to answer the question: Is There a "Retire Early" Personality Type? Based on responses to a survey posted on the Motley Fool Retire Early Home Page board, he found that the three most common types represented in the survey were ISTJ, INTJ, and INTP. Interestingly, although other sources show that ENTJs, ESTJs, and ESFJs were the top 3 in earnings, and are about 30% of the general population, they make up only about 6% of the retire early types in the survey.

Natural Killers (added 19 Apr 2000)
In Natural Killers: Turning the Tide of Battle, Major David S. Pierson, US Army describes factors that effect whether a soldier will fire (I was surprised to find out that only 15-25% of American infantry ever fired a weapon in combat in World War II) and how effective he will be. I was heartened to hear that one of the main problems with combat training is just getting soldiers to fire. And even when training got the firing rates up to 95% by the time of the Vietnam war, it still didn't ensure that the bullets would reach their targets. Major Pierson's article goes on to provide methods to identify whether a soldier is part of the 3-4% of the male and 1% of the female population who are "natural killers" - i.e., they do not suffer remorse from killing. One of the factors is Myers-Briggs™ type, which when combined with several other factors, may help identify these people. A chilling article, but interesting and appears well researched.

Myers-Briggs™ Types of Singapore Managers (added 12 Apr 2000)
In their article Fingerprinting personalities, Tan Ngoh Tiong and Vicky Tan describe a study of the Myers-Briggs™ types of 442 women and 343 men conducted by Hermann Ditzig and You Poh Seng. The types are broken down by gender, industry, and education level. The most common type among the Singapore managers was ISTJ, at 32% followed by ESTJ at 19%. Interestingly, INTP was among the four other types with 5% or more respondents.

Research of Mensa Personality Types (added 22 Mar 2000)
On her page Mensan Personality Discovery, Patricia Hedges summarizes research she has done on the personality type of people belonging to Mensa. She administered her "Personality Discovery Questionnaire" to members of Mensa on two occasions, about five years apart. The members turned out to have a much higher percentage of "IN" preferences than the general population. Interestingly, while the percentage of Mensans with a T preference was also somewhat higher than the general population, this difference was nowhere near as marked as the I-E and S-N differences. Hedges notes on another page that the test to qualify for Mensa membership "... is not based on a knowledge of facts, but on abstract reasoning - mainly the ability to explain, understand and differentiate language, and to solve mathematical and visual puzzles. A person with immense factual and general knowledge might score very low in this test. Equally, a person with poor factual and general knowledge could gain high scores." Thus, she predicts, and her research tends to corroborate, that the test would be biased toward the N preference.

Predicting Entrepreneurs (added 9 Feb 2000)
In the study, A Comparison of Four Scales Predicting Entrepreneurship, published in the Academy of Entrepreneurship Journal, Volume 1, Number 2, four scales that have been used in entrepreneurship research, including the MBTI® instrument, were compared to determine which scale or combination of scales was best at predicting whether someone was an entrepreneur or not. This study found that entrepreneurs in the sample were more likely to be N and P. There was no significant difference in the T-F functions between respondants who were and were not entrepreneurs. And as with anything with Myers-Briggs™ type, even though entrepreneurs were more likely to be NP, there were plenty of SJ entrepreneurs also.

Naturalistic vs Analytic Decision-Making in Canadian Military (added 22 Dec 1999)
A student attending the Canadian Forces College wrote a paper called Situating the Estimate: Naturalistic Decision-Making as an Alternative to Analytical Decision-making in the Canadian Forces. The paper assumes T for the decision-making function, but differentiates between naturalistic decision making (using N or intuitive information gathering) and analytic decision making (using S or detailed and exhaustive option analysis). He has an interesting way of describing the four functions: S is assessment of reality, N is vision of the future, T is logical decision making and F is value-oriented decision making.  

S College Students and N Professors (added 8 Dec 1999) 
Most college professors are IN or EN. With college campuses more closely reflecting the population at large, more of the students are ES or IS. In his article New Students - New Learning Styles, Charles C. Schroeder discusses some of the results of the TRAILS (Tracking Retention and Academic Integration by Learning Styles) study conducted at Saint Louis University. One interesting item: for the first two years of college, the highest grades are earned by IN's, followed by EN's, IS's and then ES's. However, from the junior year on, there is no difference in academic score among the types. The author conjectures that this may be because the first  two years of college tend to be general education courses not directly related to the chosen major, and thus are seen by ES and IS types as "obstacles because they have little practical utility." 

Lawyer Personality and Crises in Legal Profession (added 24 Nov 1999) 
LAWYER, KNOW THYSELF: A REVIEW OF EMPIRICAL RESEARCH ON ATTORNEY ATTRIBUTES BEARING ON PROFESSIONALISM attempts to discover whether there is a lawyer personality and whether this is related to three crises in the legal profession: "professionalism" has declined, public opinion of attorneys and the legal profession has plummeted, and lawyer dissatisfaction and dysfunction have increased." The author, Susan Daicoff, reviews a great deal of material related to personality testing and traits, not just the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator® instrument. One interesting finding: one study found that whether students dropped out was related to their type and not to their LSAT or other academic measures. ISTJ's were least likely, and ESFJ's were most likely to drop out.

Interpreters for the Deaf (added 24 Nov 1999) 
Personality Type and Interpreters for the Deaf is a study which seeks to discover which personality types become interpreters for the deaf and where they prefer to work. The predominant type of the  interpreters in the study was INFJ, although the predominant type varied according to where the interpreter chose to work.

Personality and Animal Research (added 5 Jul 1999)

Two of them strongly favor animal research, and two strongly oppose it. They surveyed 1055 college students and compared scores on the Myers-Briggs Type Inventory and other personality measures with scores on the Animal Research Survey. 

Is There a Lawyer Personality? (added 7 Jun 1999) 
Larry Richard of Altman Weil, Inc. conducted a nationwide survey of lawyers’ personality styles. In his article, The Lawyer Personality, he discusses the results of the survey. More than half of all lawyers in the survey were of just four types: ISTJ, ESTJ,  INTJ, and ENTP. The TJ preponderance didn't surprise me, but I was interested to see ENTP in the top four. Richard also goes on to relate different type combinations to different fields of law.

Mechanical Design Course Statistics (added 1 Feb 1999) 
During the spring 1996 semester, all students in MAE 225: Mechanical Design Synthesis at Cornell's Sibley School of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering were given the opportunity to take the short form self-scored version of the Myers-Briggs™ inventory. 99 students participated and reported their results for statistical purposes. The most common type was INTP at 16%, closely followed by ISTJ at 15%, and ENTP at 12%. Next were INFP and INTJ, tied at 10%. 65% of the students preferred introversion, 41% reported having the NT temperament, 30% were SJ, 17% were NF, and 11% were SP. In this week's feature, I have some comments on ISTJ's and Careers. 

A Musician Researches Educators, the Internet and Type (added 18 Jan 1999) 
Carla Piper is a music teacher and accomplished musician who has conducted a study exploring the use of the internet and the World Wide Web by educators. Out of 51 respondants to her survey of educators, the three most common types to respond were ENFP (25.5%), ESTP (17.6%) and ISTP (also 17.6%). She found that T's were most likely to have used the internet the longest, and that J's were more likely to say they use the internet for professional development as opposed to for communication. Also, people who use the internet for email are less likely to use it for news and information.

On-Line Type Data (added Fall 1998) 
Jonathan Magid compiled cumulative data of almost 2 million people who have taken the Kiersey sorter on the web. The two big surprises to me were that about 13% of the test takers were ISTJ's compared to their real world frequency of 6% of the population, and that INTP's were actually slightly underrepresented with about 4% vs their real world frequency of 5%. This compares to the data I compiled on September 14, 1998 from Doug Ingram's Personality Index of 5.2% ISTJ's 14% INTP's on his list out of a sample size of 497. 

Study Relates Partner Satisfaction, Type (added Fall 1998) 
Partner Satisfaction as Measured by the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator is a study by St. Mary's University researchers, headed by psychologist Nancy S.Marioles, Ph.D in which the researchers asked 426 married and premarried couples to take the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator® instrument. The couples were then monitored over a seven-year period to determine marital behavior and predictors of satisfaction and divorce. In this study, at least, opposite types did not attract. "The only exceptions were ESTJ men married to INFP women and ESTP men married to INFJ women." 

Counseling Difficulty (added Fall 1998) 
In the dissertation Predictive Validity of the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator Composite Preference Score to Estimate Counseling Difficulty and Counseling Outcome, Jerry L. Brinegar, Ph.D. investigates whether people with strong Myers-Briggs™ preferences (high composite preference score) are more inflexible, and thus more resistant to counseling. Dr. Brinegar's used a sample of 185 people in counseling for various reasons. The results of the study: people with strong preferences are harder to counsel, but do not have worse counseling outcomes. 

Benziger Assesses Falsifying Type (added Fall 1998) 
According to Katherine Benziger, Ph.D., there are physiological bases for the four functions (N,S,T,F), and the two orientations (I-E). Consistently using the function that is not our dominant leads to measurable physiological symptoms (such as fatigue). Dr. Benziger uses the Benziger Thinking Styles Assessment to determine your true type, and whether you are falsifying type. 

Relating and the Shadow (added Fall 1998) 
Clint Combs, an INFJ describes A Personal Encounter with the Shadow, or least preferred function (in his case, extroverted sensing). 

More Shadowing (added Fall 1998) 
Timothy Miller, INTP, has some interesting ideas on Myers-Briggs™ type. He theorizes that our type is a "synthesis" of two types he calls alpha types. He also posits an interface type, which is how we appear when we need to interact with the opposing mode of perception. According to his theory, when I as an INTP interact using S, then my behavioral style is ISFJ (I basically go into my tertiary S and inferior Fe). In his theory we do not just have shadow function but an entire shadow personality type. Interestingly, he claims that this shadow personality type is not based on the inferior function, but calculates it differently.
