Macroeconomics and Type (added 26 Jan 2000)
Mary O. Borg and Stephen L. Shapiro have conducted a study relating type and how well the students do in an introductory economics course. In Personality Type and Student Performance in Principles of Economics, they find that SJ's outperform NT's and NF's. More importantly, students who had the same temperament as the teacher did significantly than those who didn't.  
Short Course on Type On-Line (added 22 Dec 1999)
Cindy Funkhouser has created a free on-line course offered through SmartPlanet called Understanding Personality Types which provides a quick introduction to type concepts. The course walks you through a brief history of the MBTI® instrument, and has you take the Keirsey sorter. Then, which your results in hand, you are introduced to what those letters mean, first in terms of temperament, then in terms of the four-letter type. Finally, you are pointed to resources for finding others of your type on the web. This site is listed as one of the resources for the course. 
Five Assignments for Learning Style (added 1 Dec 1999)
"Learning about Learning: Intelligence, Learning, and Learning Styles" has a different way to introduce learning styles. Thomas V. Edgar, P.E., Ph.D., Department of Civil and Architectural Engineering at the University of Wyoming introduces five assignments, and then asks you to identify which you would consider easier or which you would do first. Although he discusses various learning style theories, including Myers-Briggs™ type, he never explicitly connects the five assignments with Myers-Briggs™ types, temperaments, or function combinations.
List for Teaching Composition (added 3 Nov 1999) 
The listserv, TYPWRT-L, is for discussions on Jungian psychological type & Teaching of Composition. Kairos, a Journal for Teachers of Writing in Webbed Environments, wrote a review of the list at List Review of Typwrt-L.  
Navajo College Students and Type (added 13 Oct 1999) 
In Personality Differences Between Navajo and Non-Indian College Students: Implications for Instruction, a study comparing the types of Navajo students to other students, William Huitt finds a different distribution of types between the two groups. The Navajo's are more likely to be I, S and T than the rest of the student population.  
MBTI-LAW Listserv (added 29 Sep 1999) 
This is a "Listserv for Discussion of Use of the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator in Law Schools," hosted at the Chicago-Kent College of Law. The group is a private one, in that it is limited to those who work as personnel in some capacity for a law school, such as professors, adjunct professors, career services professionals, academic support professionals and legal writing instructors.  
Using Type in Developing Characters (added 22 Aug 1999) 
Ann Hart teaches life story writing and fiction writing online at WritersWrite University. She uses the MBTI® types to develop unforgettable characters, dialogue, and choose the best-fit genre according to MBTI® type and Keirsey Temperament, etc. Her home page is at: Creativity INFP. Information about her courses are at her page at Writers Write University.  
College Simulation Includes Type (added 16 Aug 1999) 
When I first got to a page which listed college personnel broken down by race, gender, age and Myers-Briggs™ type, my first thought was stereotyping. Then I realized I had entered a simulation of a college and its administrators, and type was just one of the factors in the background information, which also included history of the college, geography of the state, layout of the campus, and many other factors. Check out Oconee College: A Simulation for the Study of Higher Education put together by the Department of Counseling and Human Development in the College of Education at the University of Georgia.

MBTI in Engineering Education contains lots of information on teaching and learning styles, whether you are interested in Engineering or not. However, it does include engineering-specific data. It also deals extensively with the S-N differences in teaching and learning.

How do you teach theory to Sensor? Try WMBK, application-theory-application, and the advance organizer. In his article, On Learning Styles, Harvey J. Brightman, Georgia State University discusses specific teaching strategies based on Myers-Briggs™ profiles.

Type Information on Military Institute Cadets (added 10 May 1999) 
The Office of Institutional Research at the Virginia Military Institute has some information on the MBTI types of their cadets. The top three most numerous profiles of new cadets are ISTJ, ESTJ and ISTP. Those with the ST type have historically left earliest in their VMI experience. But the other types who tend to drop out before graduation are ISTJ, ISFJ, ISFP and INFP. I wonder what the most common types that make it to graduation are then? Or what types have the highest percentage of that type making it to graduation.  
Student Leaders and Type (added 19 Apr 1999)  
Birmingham-Southern College is a four year liberal arts college affiliated with the United Methodist Church. One of their special programs is the Center for Leadership Studies, which includes a one week Student Leaders in Service program for high school students. Part of the program uses Myers-Briggs™ type, and each student participant has a page which includes their type and their vision of leadership.  
More on Learning Styles (added 1 Mar 1999) 
Interconnections has a site called the Mind, Body and Spirit network. They currently feature a free Learning Style Report provided by a company called Integra. You answer four questions to determine your type, and then link to a page with a description of your learning style. If you don't already know your type, I doubt you'll determine it from the simple questionnaire, but the learning style report is interesting, and seemed accurate when I took it. Of course, Integra hopes you'll also order more materials from them to help you with training and career development.  
Attention Deficit Disorder, NP's and SP's (added 22 Feb 1999) 
Born to Explore is a site which focuses on the positive aspects of ADD. The most common types among the visitors to Teresa Gallagher's site on Attention Deficit Disorder, called Born to Explore, are INFP, ENFP, INTP and ENTP in that order. She makes an interesting case for a connection between NP preferences and ADD, in addition to Kiersey's thoughts, which expects ADD to be primarily (but not always) a result of SP students in SJ schools. Teresa, an ENTP, seems to subscribe to a rather common idea that links creativity with the N preference: I believe that people with S function tend to express creativity concretely, while those with the N preference tend to express it abstractly (see my articles Sensors, Intuitives and Creativity from 1998 and Concrete Imagination from 1999). However, the difference between abstract and concrete creativity may still explain many of the problems "N" people have in a predominantly "S" world. Teresa has since emailed me that she finds more of a connection between creativity and the "P" attitude than either the "N" or "S" functions. She allowed me to publish it as a guest feature on 1 March 1999 (Features > 1999 First Quarter Features Archive).  
Color coded temperament at school (added Fall 1998) 
In Type Goes to School, from the TypeWorkS newsletter there is a description of how high school Dean Kristina Giger used temperament at her school. Instead of the usual temperament names, she uses color-coded categories that students can relate to.  
Sensing children at school (added Fall 1998) 
Mychal Wynn specializes in teacher training, parent workshops, classroom demonstrations, student presentations, team building, and leadership training. Part of his focus is on how to use knowledge of type and multiple intelligences in teaching school children. In an web page article, he gives an example of how sensing children may have a harder time when they first start school than intuitive children.  
Demonstrating S-N type differences (added Fall 1998) 
TypeWorkS has several articles online about how to demonstrate type differences. This exercise, contributed by C. Waite Maclin, ENFJ, uses tinker toys or legos to "demonstrate the effect of sensing and intuitive preferences on product and process."  
Card Game (added Fall 1998) 
Connie Busse has designed a card game that uses the four preferences as card suits. Every player gets seven cards. Each player attempts to obtain a hand including four preferences for one type, and three of the same preferences outside of that type. For example, if your hand contains  ESFP, then the other three cards would all need to be the same preference chosen from the remaining I, N, T, and J. 
First Year Law Students and Performance (added Fall 1998) 
Vernellia R. Randall, ENTJ, in The Myers-Briggs Type Indicator, First Year Law Students and Performance, discusses how first year law school is set up, not to teach students, but to "weed" them out. Reminds me of my first semester as a Physics major. She discusses the correlation between type, and success in law school, and provides type-related suggestions for improving legal pedagogy. For example, students with preferences for I and T did better in a study of first semester law students.  
Learning styles of types (added Fall 1998) 
Doug Dean's site Learning Styles of Psychological Types gives an overview of temperament, and then describes learning styles for each temperament. He also includes a survey you can participate in, where you enter your name, email, type, and some comments. Most of the entrants are N's. Wonder how you could get a survey that would encourage the S's to participate? 
Medical Students - Use Type to Improve Test Results (added Fall 1998) 
This site promotes a book, SuccessTypes for Medical Students, that describes how to apply knowledge of your preferred learning style to test taking. An exerpt from the book describes how the S-N dichotomy affects how a student takes a multiple choice test. The author stresses that your type preference does not prevent you from learning skills associated with the other function preferences. I particularly liked one of his quotes: "1.Performing in your type is play.  2.Performing in the opposite of your type is work."
