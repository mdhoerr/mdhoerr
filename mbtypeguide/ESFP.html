ESFP Home Pages 
Don's Windward Archipelago
(added 31 May 2000) 
The homepage is an image map of what looks like Carribean islands (OK, so my geography is not so hot), divided into "countries" or "regions" that make up his table of contents. In fact, at first I thought it was about travel to actual Carribean countries until I read the names of the "countries": About Me, Tropics, Bushwackers, PsiPODS, Midis, and so on. Each "country" takes you to a list of links with one-sentence descriptions in that category, in no particular order. Best use I've seen so far of midi on a site - directly relates to a major interest of his, and you don't have to wait for the midi to load before you can see the site.

Tony Malloy - Welcome to Tony's Place  
(updated 19 Jul 1999)  
First he apologizes that he hasn't updated his site in a year, then his first link is his guest book - "Sign-in", followed by "Family and Friends". Note: In Doug's index, there are fewer home pages listed for this type than any other.  
Susan Carle Young   
(updated 24 May 1999)  
She has updated her home page. Her favorite type profiles on-line are from The Personality Page. The main difference is the arrangement of her site into categories: Pictures, Literary, Music, Travel, Web Stuff, Personality Types, "Cooking, Gardens, Genealogy, Politics, Movies, Medical Research", Spiritual Resources. Yes, the category in quotes is one category. Having only one or two links is what unites Cooking, Gardens, Genealogy, Politics, Movies and Medical Research into one category. Travel includes a fascinating link to Dangerous Places to travel. And as before, links to pictures and sites of friends and family make up the largest single category.  
Welcome to a Cyber-denizen's Nook   
(added 2 August 1999)  
is the name of Suman Chepuri's personal web pages. His page is attractive and chatty, introducing you to him, his school, and his interests as if you were sitting across the table from him. Although it was difficult for me to try to describe the organization of the personal pages, they did not seem random or disjointed. Looks good, and works well, but I can't analyze why! Does have a lot of Java, though, which sometimes breaks.
