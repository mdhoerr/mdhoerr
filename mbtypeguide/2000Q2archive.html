Fe vs Fi: a Fictional Case Study
by Mary Hoerr, June 28, 2000

Modern movies seem to stress the importance of subjective Feeling over objective Feeling. The bad guys are the parents that want to arrange a marriage; the good guys are the star-crossed lovers. The Richard Rich video, The Swan Princess, is different in giving equal weight to both aspects.
The widowed parents of Derek and Odette decide they want their children to marry, so that the two kingdoms can be united. This is clearly an extraverted Feeling goal. The Feeling value is the uniting of the two kingdoms, and it doesn't particularly matter that Derek is a little boy and Odette still a baby. However, rather than directly impose their judgement on the children Queen Uberta (Derek's mother) and King William (Odette's father) hope that the children will fall in love if they are just brought together every summer and get to know each other.
Summer after summer the two grow up from small children to pre-teens to teenagers, during which time "the only thing on which they didn't disagree was that the very thought of summertime was dreaded." The scenes of the constant sparring are hilarious, but there is an underlying wisdom to the approach. Subjective feeling, Fi, when undeveloped, just tells us what we like or don't like, and the two children are quite vocal about that. But if Fi is to reach the ability to identify likes and dislikes with universal human value, it needs data or experience. The summers together provide just the experience needed to inform developing Fi.
The result is a first for fairy tales, as far as I know. As young adults, the two finally realize they have fallen in love. Fe values and Fi values have come into perfect alignment. Tongue-tied Derek doesn't know what to say, and ends up giving an answer that sounds like all he cares about is Odette's beauty. Derek, the only fairy tale prince who actually knows the beautiful princess before falling in love with her, gets himself accused of not caring about her as a person!
Although Queen Uberta and King William have cleverly arranged things as best they could to get the two to fall in love, when Odette makes the decision not to marry Derek, they honor the subjective Feeling values represented. Marriage for beauty alone is not acceptable, and other arrangements will now have to be made to assure the next generation of rulers in both kingdoms. Then, the main part of the video begins.

Feeling - Inside and Out
by Mary Hoerr, June 21, 2000

The feeling function makes rational decisions based on value criteria. We tend to think of "feeling" as being subjective. But according to type theory, this is only true of introverted feeling. Value judgements can be based on either objective or subjective criteria, where objective means "existing in the outer world" and subjective means "experienced in the inner world". If that sounds like the difference between extraverted and introverted, you're right. Extraverted Feeling is objective, Introverted Feeling is subjective.
According to Lenore Thomson (ref) "Introverted Feeling relies on the inward, right-brain criteria of experience and empathy to mark off decisions that go beyond our roles in society to affect as a human beings."
It's easy to see how value judgements can be subjective. Introverted Feeling, the dominant functions for ISFP and INFP types, determines the value of something by our inner feelings about it, rather than by social norms. On the mundane level, Fi just tells us whether we're happy or sad about something, whether we like it or not. On a more serious level, Fi tells us that it is more important to attend a funeral and express grief in whatever clothes we have, than to stay away just because we don't have the right color costume. The drawback of Fi is that it can mistake mere personal preference (I like this kind of music) for universal human values (this is the only good kind of music). At its best, it connects us to univeral human values (music is good).
According to Lenore Thomson (ref) "Extraverted Feeling relies on the outward, left-brain criteria of custom and law to mark off decisions that go beyond our immediate experience to affect the larger community."
Extraverted Feeling, the dominant function of ESFJ and ENFJ types, is objective. How can value judgements be objective? When they are based on collective social norms. People don't follow social norms primarily because they "feel like it", but because they want to show how they value something in a way most people will understand. On the mundane level, dressing up for an occassion shows what kind of value you place on it. How you dress up for any specific occassion will depend on your culture. For example, a Chinese person may wear white to a funeral, while an American will wear black. On a more serious level, Fe tells us it is wrong to go back on a contractual agreement, even if we know we would never get caught. Our personal wrongdoing can have important consequences to the community at large. The drawback of Fe is that we can mistake the form of the social norm (wear black to funerals to show mourning) with the spirit of the social norm (show and share your grief in community). At its best, it connects us to the important values of our culture.

The Right and the Left
by Mary Hoerr, June 14, 2000

Are some functions "right brain or left brain" functions? Are some types? According to Thomson (ref 1) and Niednagel (ref 2), the answer to the first question is a qualified no, and to the second is yes. All four functions, S, N, F and T are found on both sides of the brain. However, the introverted and extraverted form of each function are on opposite sides of the brain.
The left side of the brain tends to process information linearly. "The left brain processes information one thing at a time. If it has to deal with many issues, it will organize them sequentially or numerically." (ref 1) This side of the brain is where the introverted forms of S and N tend to reside, as well as the extraverted forms of T and F.
The right side of the brain tends to process information holistically. "The right brain is designed to process many things at once and to apprehend them in whole patterns. It does not recognize sequence or abstraction." (ref 1) This side of the brain is where the introverted forms of T and F tend to reside, as well as the extraverted forms of S and N.
Interestingly, if you look at the functions of each type, you will notice that the dominant and auxiliary function of each type are activated on the same side of the brain. Those types which prefer to extravert their perceiving function (P preference) use the right side of the brain for their dominant and auxiliary: ISTP, ESTP, ISFP, ESFP, INTP, ENTP, INFP, ENFP. Those types which prefer to extravert their judging function (J preference) use the left side: ISTJ, ESTJ, ISFJ, ESFJ, INTJ, ENTJ, INFJ, ENFJ.
By the way, this method of mapping type functions to areas of the brain is different from Dr. Benziger's method. She locates N and F in the right brain, and T and S in the left brain. (ref 3). Rather than going strictly by the dominant and auxiliary functions, she assesses which quadrants of the brain you tend to activate most: some people are Quarter Brain Experts (25%), most are Half Brainers (55%), some are Triple Brainers (15%) and a few are Whole Brainers (5%). Roughly speaking, if Half Brain dominance correlates to dominant and auxiliary functions, then all NF's are right brainers, all ST's are left brainers, and NT's and SF's use a quadrant from each side of the brain.

The Typology of Autism, Part 1
by Mary Hoerr, May 31, 2000

I have just finished reading Dr. Temple Grandin's book, Thinking in Pictures: And Other Reports From My Life With Autism, about her experiences and thought processes. Although the book never refers to type, it was impossible for me to read without thinking of type. I've often thought that descriptions of Asperger's syndrome, a from of high functioning autism, which is what Dr. Grandin has, sounded a bit like some of the INTP descriptions I've read.
Reading her book, and re-reading Personality Type: An Owner's Manual, and Your Key to Sports Success, it occurred to me that one aspect of autism may be problems with access to the extroverted parts of the brain.
One aspect of autism appears to be sensory processing problems. Autistic people show immature development of the cerebellum, part of which acts as a volume control for the senses, and of the brain stem, which also has some role in sensory processing. Thus, the senses may not be interpreted or processed correctly in some autistic people. The input they get from their senses ranges from mild oversensitivity to jumbled, inaccurate information, both visually and aurally. Dr. Grandin states: "Those who escape the prison of low-functioning autism probably do so because just enough undistorted information gets through. They do not totally lose contact with the world around them." pp 52, 53 [emphasis mine] I wonder whether this may also indicate difficulty in accessing extraverted functions, which by definition deal with the outer world.
The introverted functions are in the posterior of the forebrain. This part of the forebrain gathers and handles input from the outside world, received via the senses, and translates this input into meaning. Specifically, the primary visual cortex of this part of the brain is where the process of visual imaging begins. The parietal cortex, also part of the posterior of the forebrain, integrates sensory inputs, handling spatial relations among parts of the body and between the body and objects of the outside world.
Problems with sensory processing may have several effects. First, the autistic person may need to spend more time with sensory processing - ie, with introverted functions. This may be a factor in the difficulty autistic people have with changing focus. Second, the information that the extraverted portions of the brain have to work with may be suspect. This may lead to hesitation in taking action in the outside world, and may be a factor in lack of flexibility and difficulty with change.
Is there a relation between how autistic brain functions look, as described by Dr. Grandin, and the various type functions? From her book, it is obvious that her dominant function appears to be Ti. Next week I plan to go through how the other functions looked to me as I went through her book.

A Sensual Experience
by Mary Hoerr, May 24, 2000
reprinted from October 19, 1998

Several times in my life I have experienced something I came to call "clear sight". No, it had nothing to do with clairvoyance or anything psychic. In fact, just the opposite. I called it "clear sight" because I was able to "see" (and hear and sense) things clearly as they actually are. 
One experience was when I was practicing soccer. There was a measureless and miraculous moment when I clearly saw where all the players were, and knew exactly where the ball was going and how I needed to physically move myself to get to the ball and gain control. I don't remember whether I actually got the ball - sensing and doing are two different things - but I clearly saw all the sensory factors of the game. 
Please understand that I am not talking about predicting or seeing what wasn't there. I was seeing precisely and only what was there. Sensors will immediately know what I'm talking about, but I imagine they will wonder what I'm making such a big deal about. 
Sometimes we think of intuitives as "creative" and sensors as grounded in the "here and now", but I think that misses something. The experience of "clear sight" has occurred only a few times in my life, bright and vivid in my memory. I can't help but think that it must be the usual way for a mature sensor to see the world.

Misuse of Type: What Happened 
by Mary Hoerr, 17 May 2000

Here is a story about the misuse of type at work by a visitor to the site. To protect anonymity, I have used "Company A" and "Company B" to refer to the organizations in the article. Note: neither organization currently has a link review on this site.

I worked at a major corporation (Company A) as a Total Quality Management (TQM) consultant. The new general manager at our facility had been exposed to type information at his last position, and he wanted to do the same again. Before I arrived, he had his former consultant visit our facility. They used the information collected to threaten the managers into compliance with his new "TQM" program. Needless to say, by the time I arrived, the managers and staff were very hostile to me and to any type of Organizational Development (OD) intervention.
I was sent to a class that the consultant and others at Company B had developed. After the first couple of days, I registered complaints about how the information was being used in Company A. Personal information derived from the classes was used against people when they returned to their worksite. It was suggested that the source of my complaining was due to my "borderline" condition and the observation that I was "just not getting it". I was kicked out of class for "not getting it". I was reinstated by the director of Company B after explaining to him some of the political situation, as long as I agreed not to talk in the class.
When I got back to work (this class was held over several months, with breaks in between), I started administering Ned Hermann's Whole Brain "test" (HBDI) in my division. Several people did not want to take the test, because they thought that it would be used against them. I asssured them that our division was not going to participate in those types of activities going on at Company B. Later, I became very concerned when the General Manager mentioned that he was going to fire another manager because he was a feeling type. He said, "That type of behavior may be OK for my wife, but not for a manager here at _______!!" When I went back to the Company B, all hell broke loose (OK, I refused to keep quiet!). I also scheduled an evening meeting with the VP of corporate HR at Company A, to tell him what was going on. I was especially concerned about the potential removal of personality types that have a gender component, (like T-F) which I consider to be a serious discriminiation issue.
When I finished the class, I gave the rest of the HBDI forms to staff. I coached several very frightened employees on how to fudge the scores to come out with a higher A and lower C quadrant score (which correspond to Jung's Thinking and Feeling types). Later that week, I was fired "for being a feeling type". I was told that the only way to get through to these managers, "and their projecting" was "to bust them over the heads with it", and clearly I was not up to that. I threw the completed HBDI forms in the trash.
After this experience I got out of consulting altogether and I do whatever I can to make sure that these techinques are never used in industry!!!!!
Note: I have done the official MBTI "test" twice and have come out as ENTJ and ENTP. My "coach" at Company B, said it was more likely that I was an INTJ.

What Test (Indicator) Should I Take? 
by Mary Hoerr, July 12, 1999, updated 10 May 2000

From time to time, I get email asking me about Myers-Briggs™ testing. Before I continue, I must make two important points.
First of all, any questionnaire, sorter, or indicator based on Myers-Briggs™ should not be considered a "test." This is an important point, because I don't trust any MB-based instrument that doesn't make this clear. Most sites have to put the word "test" on their pages somewhere because people new to MB always search on "test" - which is why this article uses "test." But somewhere, prominantly the site should always tell you this is not a test and there are no right or wrong answers, just like there's no such thing as a right or wrong hair color or town of birth.
Secondly, you must know that I am not certified to administer the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator ® (MBTI®). My knowledge comes from reading and having taken the MBTI® instrument at a workshop. The theory behind the MBTI® instrument goes back to Jung. If you want to get technical, check out C. G. Jung, Analytical Psychology, and Culture,  the Association for Psychological Type or the Center for Applications of Psychological Type, Inc. 
The most certain way to determine your type is to take the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator® (MBTI® instrument) from someone who is licensed to administer it. The fee should always include a certain amount of consulting and interpretation with you after you take the indicator. You normally have to be certified to administer the indicator before you can get a copy of it. For information on getting certified, go to right hand column of Net Links > tests.
I list two [as of 10 May 2000, there were five] companies that administer the MBTI® instrument online at the top of Net Links > tests. Since prices may change, you will need to go to the site of each organization listed to get the latest price. Prices of anywhere from $50 to $150 would not be unreasonable, since they include at least one hour (and sometimes more) of professional counselling on the results of the indicator. 
The other questionnaires listed on my tests page are based on Myers-Briggs™ theory, or Keirsey's version of the Myers-Briggs™ theory. They can be taken on-line for no fee or a very small fee, but they are not as reliable, and do not include any professional interpretation and counselling on the results. Finally, the Benziger Thinking Styles Assessment (BTSA) also builds on Myers-Briggs™ theory, and includes additional analysis to determine if you may be falsifying your type. 
If you want to get reasonable certainty that you know your type, expect to pay at least $30, probably more, for the MBTI® instrument or the BTSA to be professionally administered. If you're just interested in type, you may want to start out by reading a couple of books on type, and take several of the free on-line sorters to get started. My favorite free online sorter right now is the MMDI available online at the Team Technology site. The most popular sorter is probably the Keirsey Temperament Sorter.

Hardwired Leadership Book Review

by Mary Hoerr, May 3, 2000  
Hardwired Leadership: Unleashing the Power of Personality to Become a New Millennium Leader, by Roger R. Pearman, describes leadership as a psychological process that is influenced by the "habits of mind" that each leader has. These "habits of mind" are defined by our type. He then shows how each leadership process, from "communication" to "problem management" and more, are affected by these habits of mind.
Pearson provides a lot of detailed information about each type's approach to many leadership issues. In the section discussing communication, for example, he provides a short description of the General Style, Potential Miscues, Core Beliefs and Reality Checks (kinds of information that type tends to discount) for each habit of mind. Thus, an INTP's questioning might be a miscue that is seen by others as disrespect, while an ESFJ's sympathetic attitude might be misread as indicating agreement. An ENFP might need to consider conventional answers to provide a reality check, while an ISTJ might need to consider emotional responses.
The book is full of such matrices for many aspects of leadership. What does each habit of mind trust or mistrust? The INFP expects ideas to be meaningful, and distrusts actions that look harmful to others. The ESTJ trusts facts and past experience, and doubts the ability of those who do not provide a thorough analysis. What does fairness look like to each type? Dependability? Supportiveness? A supportive ISTP will make candid statements about what is currently happening. That doesn't look supportive to an ISFP, who is looking for demonstrations of caring. And there are many more charts for other processes.
An interesting one to me was the section on blind spots. One chart shows the qualities that each type sees in itself. A second shows which of those qualities that observers also see in that type. And the last chart shows qualities that observers don't see that the type thinks it has. For example, an INTP may see herself as independent, flexible and good at interpersonal relations. Observers see her (and she also sees herself) as decisive, independent, a good delegater and a quick study. But they don't see her as flexible, or able to follow-through to make a good impression.
Besides the charts and matrices, there are several case studies that show typical business interactions without knowledge of type, and how those interactions can be modified by understanding types, or "habits of mind."

Misuse of Type in Work, Stereotypes
by Mary Hoerr, April 26, 2000 

If type theory is used properly, it seems to me that most of the time it should improve the work environment by showing people other ways of relating to the world. For example, it would show folks with the S preference that N is not flighty, folks with E that I's aren't suspicious and secretive, etc.
Yet there is great concern. One should differentiate between function capabilities needed for a job (ie, logical analysis, people skills, etc) and the types of people who apply for the job. It may make sense to analyse a team and say the new hire should be good at "N" or "S" or whatever. In fact, type theory may open the mind of management to the fact that N or F or P capabilities are actually important.
But this is entirely different from saying you should hire someone with those preferences. Someone with an S preference may very well have highly developed N - in fact, may have more developed N than someone with a natural N preference.
Just because you have a preference does not necessarily mean you are good at that function. For many reasons, you may actually have developed another function to a higher level than your preferred function.
And what about stereotyping? Doesn't everyone have a unique personality, not just one predetermined one out of 16 possible? Isn't it psychologically damaging to impose a stereotype on people?
Of course, everyone has a unique personality, just as everyone has a unique fingerprint. And yet, fingerprints can be classified according to certain general characteristics. Every INTP is like every other INTP in some ways, and completely different in other ways. Strength of preferences, development of nonpreferences, and many other factors go to make up our personality.
As for stereotyping, any classification scheme is open to that. Yet the question as to whether the MBTI® instrument classification system does more harm than good is an ongoing one that I wrestle with.
On the one hand, people tend to believe that the way they perceive the world is the correct one. If the MBTI® instrument can open them up to other equally valid ways, then it is useful. If it merely provides another excuse to discount what those "crazy N's" or "uncreative S's" are saying, then it is damaging.

Career and the Other Functions
by Mary Hoerr, April 19, 2000 

You may want to determine your own dominant, auxiliary, tertiary and inferior functions from the Types page on this site.
One aspect of the type development is that career goals can change as a person reaches different life stages. Jack Falt has a nice little "play" called What's going On? The Play that Never Ends that illustrates about when each function develops. During adolescence, the focus is more on the auxiliary. Then, in young adulthood (about 20 - 35 years old), the tertiary is developed. Finally, the inferior starts getting the focus, about the time some folks have a mid-life crisis. Please note that these ages are very *rough* guidelines. Also note that my understanding is that people do not usually develop the tertiary, and certainly not the inferior, in the same way and to the same extent as the dominant function - and some experts aren't even sure it would be healthy if they did.
What does this mean to your career search? Well, career goals and focus may very well change as a your career progresses. During the initial job search phase, from late adolescence to early adulthood, you will usually want something in line with your dominant and auxiliary functions. Perhaps you will have some interest in the tertiary as well. As an INTP, this means the first jobs I were attracted to involved science, especially in the aerospace industry (which appealed to both Ne and Ti). My tertiary didn't interest me much, although I used it to get involved in some computer support (which back in the mid 80's was something reserved for clerical support and lowly second lieutenants).
Now in my 40's, I'm more interested in developing more Fe capability. The quality of a 20-year-old INTP doing end-user support, and a 40-year-old is striking. The main difference I can see is simply more development of Fe. I think I am just better at communicating with people and understanding their point of view than I ever was when I was younger. I don't feel as technically sharp though.
What this means is that what kind of job you're looking for depends as much as your stage in life as on your type. You may very well be interested in a job that draws more on your tertiary or inferior function. So let the various type lists be a guide and give you ideas, but don't let them dictate what you should do!

The Other Functions
by Mary Hoerr, April 12, 2000 

A type is more than four letters. And even though every type only contains two functions (ST, SF, NT or NF) in the type name, the other two functions also play a role. Each type has a tertiary and an inferior function, that includes those other two. The tertiary and inferior function for each type is listed on the Types page on this site. For this article, I will illustrate examples using my own type of INTP. You may want to note down the dominant, auxiliary, tertiary and inferior functions for your own type, and consider your own case as you read the examples. For most of this feature, I draw on my understanding of what Lenore Thomson writes in Personality Type: An Owner's Manual (*Read my Review * Buy it at Amazon*).
Recently, I have become fascinated by the different roles the tertiary and inferior function play. When we use our dominant function too much, we lose balance between the inner and outer worlds. Here, "balance" doesn't mean fifty-fifty, but simply enough recognition of the attitude, or world, that is not dominant. When this happens, we should use the auxiliary function (which always has the opposite attitude to the dominant) to bring us into better balance. To do this, the auxiliary function cannot be used in its usual role of supporting the dominant function, but needs to "become" dominant.
In other words, an INTP can use dominant Ti so much that she doesn't maintain enough perspective from the outer world. Instead of just using Ne to gather more "stuff" for Ti to analyze, she needs to view herself as one of the elements in the *outer* world that Ne perceives.
When a type becomes unbalanced, the inferior tries to pull it back. But the person is likely to misread the messages as an outer phenomenon, instead of a message for balance. When Fe sends messages to an INTP to look for the appreciation and approval of others in the *outer* world, she is likely to feel instead that "nobody understands or cares about me."
The tertiary function then has an interesting role. Usually, we use it to add richness to our lives, by focussing on things we usually overlook. But when we're trying to avoid the messages from the inferior, we use the tertiary to help us fight or run from those messages. Thus, for the INTP, the tertiary function Si keeps us focussed on our inner world, and convinces us even further that no one can understand our unique inner world, or appreciate us.
When we use the auxiliary function to try to restore the balance, however, the tertiary function can take on yet another role. When the INTP uses Ne to see herself as an element to be considered, she notes that what she does effects how others perceive her and interact with her, and modify her behavior accordingly. She can then use Si, or her own unique inner picture of the world, to identify when she has reached the limits to her accommodation to the outside world.
The tertiary dynamic then is this:
When our outer and inner worlds are in balance, it provides more richness in our life by focussing on what we often overlook. When they are not, it colludes with the dominant function to try to fight or run away from the message the inferior is sending us. But when we use our auxiliary function to restore the inner/outer balance, the tertiary function provides a check, letting us know when we have reached our limits.

Type in School / Career Counseling
by Mary Hoerr, April 5, 2000 

Surfing for personality sites, I occasionally come across career or school sites. Usually, knowledge of personality type is used to help someone who is undecided to try to decide which career path to take, or to help the person develop a study or job search program. However, sometimes I come across sites that imply that personality type should be used to screen people out of certain areas.
One site showed research into the types that did well at a particular school, which had a program focussed on math, science, engineering and research. Not surprisingly, it found that students with the SP temperament did not do as well as either SJs or NTs (although the xNTPs didn't do as well as the other NTs). You may want to browse through my link reviews on teaching for more information on the problems SPs tend to have with educational institutions. However, the study went on to suggest that SP types may wish to reconsider joining the program because that temperament had a low success rate. This is especially ironic, since according to other research (see Features > 1999 Third Quarter Features Archive: Is the IT Type IT?) the ISTP type, which is SP temperament, is *most* suited for Information Technology (computer-related) jobs.
At another site, an article mentioned various testing measures used to help students decide on their career path. Presented as a kind of success story was how a student was dissuaded from a pursuing a computer science degree because a test (not a personality test) showed that a high school student had no ability in working with three-dimensional concepts. The student was shocked at the test results, because he was good at taking things apart. He wanted to become a computer programmer, and thought he was good at design. Fortunately, the kid knows better now, and is pursuing a different course of study that uses other aptitudes.
In the article referenced above, Is the IT Type IT?, the other IT types are ISTJ, INTP and INTJ. I would like to note that many type professionals, including Lenore Thomson and Jonathan Niednagel (see Net Links > books), consider the J types, including ISTJ and INTJ, to be left-brained types that do not have a natural aptitude for three-dimensional thinking. Obviously, there must be much more involved in computer programming than that.
And you can't even say that F types shouldn't go into computer science. Andrew Shulak's research on the types that go into Computer Science (unfortunately no longer posted on the web), found that the second most common type at his school was ENFP.
Interestingly enough, the first school would discourage SPs, and noted that NPs didn't do as well in their program. Yet according to type theory, it is the SPs and NPs that would be best at three-dimensional thinking. Can't win for losing.

Feedback question: 
How has knowledge of type affected your own career path? Or how do you think it should be used in job counselling?
INTP
The examples you cited illustrate well why I have severe reservations about it. I am a believer in unorthodox choices and personal growth in less natural preferences. Personally the knowledge about type has helped me to understand why I have been drawn to different roles, subjects and occupations. It all makes more sense and now I can be more clear about what I want to do and where I want to go. However, if I had known as an 18-years-old that I was INTP and immediately applied for computer science or law, I would have missed so much valuable experience.
I'm so strong N that I'm almost deficient in anything concrete. However, I spent 5 years of my life as a roulette/black jack dealer and eventually became very good in it. It was so satisfying to see that I CAN learn something where you have to use your hands and notice immediate realities. It may take more time than many others have to spend but in the end I can become just as skilled as them. This knowledge has given me confidence to tackle other stuff (like, driving car, using different kind of machines, learning to dance) where I used to think I'm hopeless.
Also I spent some time as communications professional and journalist, which is more of a people job. While I found myself avoiding face-to-face interactions and learning about technology in unnecessary depth, I still got to practice being with people and taking them into account. Now I am a happy systems engineer, but wouldn't change anything about my professional history.
People shouldn't let their type discourage them from trying challenging stuff. There are many professions where both polarities can be as necessary (for example, I think in S/N are equally valuable in designing systems). Also, people can and do enjoy jobs traditionally recommended to some other type (I know many NFs who are happy working with technology - they often have kind of vision for what to use it for).
INFP
I'm an INFP and 19 years old and am in limbo on deciding what to do (aren't we all?). Whereas a Sensor might be overwhelmed by the possibilities of what field to go to, there are very few careers out there that would let me utilize my type to the fullest extent. There's not too many other jobs besides artist, counseler/psychologist, or religious worker that are recommended for INFPs. Ultimately, I think it is totally absurd, getting a career and all. It makes me feel like a cog in a machine, having to do a specific task for the good of society. Even if it is inspiring work which I love, I would still be chained to my mandated existence. So, I guess the only thing my type has taught me about career choice is whichever I choose I'm submitting myself to becoming a robot.
