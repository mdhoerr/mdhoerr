Problems with the MBTI® Instrument in HR (added 13 Sep 2000)
In this article The Myers-Briggs Type Indicator in HRM Practice: Proceed with Caution, Michael Chase of Quincy University points out deficiencies in the instrument that Human Resources professionals should be aware of. While not discounting the instrument altogether, he points out that it oversimplifies important information, and should be balanced with the use of other pschometric instruments and conceptual schemes.

Inc.com Articles on MBTI® Instrument Use in Companies (added 19 Jul 2000)
In Inc.com article Personality Plus, two companies describe how they use personality surveys. Aken, CEO of Syncro Technology, doesn't use the MBTI® instrument in hiring and recruitment. He thinks it is "most valuable after an employee is brought on board." That article is also included in a more detailed article called Pyscho Path which has more company examples, and also includes examples of the Predictive Index, which is not related to the Myers-Briggs™ instrument.

CPP Introduces New Online Resource for HR (added 28 June 2000)
I know lots of you are HR folks, so I'm including this link even though it doesn't directly relate to Myers-Briggs™ type. The SkillsOne site includes Human Resources information, and allows you to take the Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument (TKI) online, for a fee of course.

Problem Solving Process Incorporates All Type Aspects (added 28 June 2000)
Bill Huitt describes a decision-making process that incorporates all aspects of type: E, I, S, N, F, T, P and J, in his article Problem Solving and Decision Making: Consideration of Individual Differences Using the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator. This is more than the Z method discussed by Isabel Briggs Myers in Gifts Differing (Buy it at Amazon ), which suggests using the functions in this order: S, N, T and F. Huitt also includes the E-I and P-J dichotomies. He discusses the particular problem-solving methods that seem most congenial to each function, attitude and orientation, summarized in easy-to-read tables.

Fast Company Discovers Its Type (added 21 June 2000)
The July 2000 issue of FastCompany has an article called Companies Are People Too. Regular visitors to this website heard about Sandy Fekete's Companies Are People Too back on Aug 2, 1999, when I reviewed the site. On August 16, 1999 I ran an article What Type Is This Site? about this site's type, where I mused that Myers-Briggs™ Personality Type on the Web was ENFJ, even though I am INTP. As for Fast Company's type, they list the "prayer" for their type as "Lord, help me to keep my mind on one thing -- look, a bird! -- at a time." The link review of the MBTI® type prayers was first posted in Fall 1998. And no, I won't make you surf there. Fast Company is ENFP - of course.

Writing Styles by Type (added 14 Jun 2000)
Ranshaw Consulting Inc. helps you to communicate better. One section of their site, called The Write Type, analyses writing style by type. This includes a description of where your strengths and weaknesses are likely to be. An excerpt from the section on INTP: "At their best, incisive, original writers who cut to the chase ... may resort to an overly academic style."

Types in Management (added 14 Jun 2000)
In his article, Types in Management, Steve Iman, Professor of MHR at the College of Business Administration of Cal Poly Pomona, provides statistics on the proportion of each type in management, and then discusses how type may relate to management style, organizational structure, and other areas. He also discusses the forms creativity takes for the types according to the function pairs NT, NT, ST and SF. He does not use the temperament preference pairs.

Keep your Employees from Leaving (added 7 Jun 2000)
In his article, Solving the current employee-retention problem , David J. Specht discusses how understanding the core needs of each type will help keep employees satisfied. Pay increases are only effective for 9 to 12 months after an employee has decided to leave. Deal with the type issues driving them away, or they'll be gone.

Using Type to Sell (added 31 May 2000)
SST(TM) Successful Selling to Type is a method of selling based on understanding type. You don't have to know your prospect's type. But you do need to know yours, so that you can identify the areas you are likely to push too hard or leave out. In his newsletter article Balance & Shade, Tilden makes the point that we use all our functions in making sales decisions. That said, like Brock (see link review), he identifies the important differences in making the sale to be the function pairs (ST, SF, NT and NF) rather than temperament. Of course, he's also in business to sell consulting and programs based on his technique.

Company Uses Type Knowledge in Team Building (added 24 May 2000)
Matthews Media Group has been experiencing rapid growth. In a Fast Company interview with president Molly Matthews, she describes one aspect of how she builds teamwork. "One exercise that every employee goes through when they start is the Myers-Briggs inventory, developed in the 1920s to help understand individual work styles and differences. It has contributed to a better understanding of the skills and talents each team member brings to MMG." The rest of the interview deals with other aspects of her company's challenges and successes.

Pitfalls in Using Type for Hiring (added 10 May 2000)
You know you shouldn't use type in the hiring decision, but can list the reasons clearly and concisely? Check out Brian Jones' article, Should I Use the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator® to Hire People? In eight straightforward bulleted paragraphs, he gives reasons such as "If a job candidate knew psychological type was one hiring criterion, a person could skew his or her answers to report a more desirable type for the job." or that you may be open to legal action if you do.

Team Myths and Myers-Briggs™ Type (added 22 Mar 2000)
Four Myths about Teams is a chapter reprinted online from the book WHY TEAMS DON'T WORK, by Harvey Robbins and Michael Finley at Finley's site, called mfinley.com. One of the four myths is "The Myth of Personality Type", which he says may be useful for providing "exhilarating new insights into themselves", but he says that it is irrelevant to improving teams, because this does not relate to the characteristic ways people on teams get into trouble. He is not convinced that [MBTI®] "personality reliably and consistently reveals itself in outside behaviors ... There are too many confounding life experiences which modify what we are into how we behave."

Software Designed by Ns, Used by Ss (added 2 Feb 2000)
The article Listen to Your Intuition, by the Damar Group, Ltd, points out that much (most?) software is appears to be developed to easy to use for those with an N preference. However, most software users today reflect the distribution in the population, where people with the S preference predominate. The article discusses how this may impact software user interface design, and the way software users learn new software.

Using Myers-Briggs™ Type to Help IS with Teams, Users (added 19 Jan 2000)
In his ComputerWorld article a mutual understanding, David Weldon describes how various companies are introducing their Information Systems people to Myers-Briggs™ type to help them understand end users and other team members better. Since IS people are more likely to prefer T (and be introverted) they may have more trouble overcoming distrust from managers and other employeesm partly because " ... when IS professionals discuss how a task needs to be accomplished, they tend to be poor at verbalizing how the task affects the people involved."

Interaction of Leaders and Corporate Culture (added 13 Oct 1999)

Entrepreneurs and Type Preferences (added 1 Sep 1999) 
In their study, Seeing What's Not There: the Enigma of Entrepreneurship, JoAnn C. Carland, James W. Carland and Wayne H. Stewart try to discover consistent characteristics of entrepreneurs. An interesting finding from the study sample: "The greater the level of intuition, the stronger the need for achievement and preference for innovation. However, stronger sensing preference is associated with higher risk taking propensity." This study is from the Small Business Advancement National Center at the University of Central Arkansas. 

Study on Dislocated Entrepreneurs (added 1 Sep 1999) 
In their study, Dislocation and Potential Entrepreneurship, Robert Barbato and Jeffrey Bracker discuss characteristics of "dislocated entrepreneurs" (people who seek to start their own business upon losing their job) vs traditional entrepreneurs. Type is included as one of the comparisons. This study is from the Small Business Advancement National Center at the University of Central Arkansas.

Type and Entrepreneurship (added 10 May 1999) 
After getting an email asking for more information on this topic, I dug up some interesting links. As usual, the TypeWorkS (see link review in Net Links > epubs) newsletter articles online make a good start, with an article called "ENTREPRENEURS: To be or not to be - does type make a difference?" Referenced in the article is work done by Robert Blumenthal, who found that most businesses are started by six personality types: NTJ, NTP, STJ, (E and I). Charles Ginn and Donald Sexton found that the NT (J or P) combination consistently appears in entrepreneurs. They authored "1. Growth: A Vocational Choice and Psychological Preference, Charles W. Ginn and Donald L. Sexton.", referenced at the Babson College Frontiers of Entrepreneurship Research page. James Reynierse sees the P preference as fundamental to entrepreneurism. He has authored "An MBTI® model of entrepreneurism and bureaucracy: The psychological types of business entrepreneurs compared to business managers and executives. Journal of Psychological Type, 40, 3-19." which is referenced in the CAPT library. 

Customer Support Reps and Type (added 26 Apr 1999, updated 10 May 2000) 
As of 10 May 2000, this site was no longer available.
In How to Find the Very Best Support Reps, [to get to the article, pull up their archives by clicking here, then scroll down to the Support Magazine archive, then click on the March 1998 issue, then click on Features, then click on the article "How to Find the Very Best Support Reps".] Leonard Lopez of Support Magazine, now called Customer Support Management, points out traits that are important in technical customer support reps in general, and how various companies try to choose the reps that can do the job, and fit in with the company's particular culture. The Myers-Briggs™ instrument is used sometimes, although Bill Sheehan, a Renaissance Partners consultant, notes: "If you've got a strong team culture, you've got to use tools that reveal learning and personality styles. But it's important to use such tests as a part of the process, not for eliminating candidates at the onset." 

Systems Development and Type (added 26 Apr 1999)  
In her article, Personality and System Development: What's the Connection?, Elizabeth Buie (see Focus on Type below) discusses the implication of type for System Developers. She shows statistics on type preferences among System Developers compared to the general populations, and then points out the implications - both for communication among System Developers, and for communication between System Developers and the people who use their systems. You might also want to check out my link review of Computer Science Types at Net Links > Research. Also, ESTJ David Moore (Net Links > Homes) thinks most computer types are ESTJ or ISTJ. Maybe there's a difference between system developers and system admin types?

Not Everyone who Makes it in the Military is an ISTJ (added Fall 1998) 
In the article UNCLE SAM WANTS ALL TYPES from the TypeWorkS Newsletter, Dr. McAleer discusses his experience as an INFJ and a jet pilot. Type and the Buying Decision (added Fall 1998) 
In her article "Talk to Me in My Language" from the TypeworkS newsletter, Susan A. Brock, Ph.D., ENTP, describes what people look for when they are making a buying decision. Instead of using temperament, she uses function pairs (ST, SF, NT, NF) to determine strategies for selling.
