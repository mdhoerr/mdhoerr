What Will I Do With My Money? Book Review

by Mary Hoerr, September 27, 2000  
What Will I Do With My Money?: How Your Personality Affects Your Financial Behavior, by Ray Linder Click here for more info on this or other books Back on July 12, I did a link review about Ray Linder's Good Stewardship site and money personality (see Net Links > etc.). He got in touch with me, and asked if he could send me this book. I agreed, and after reading it decided to do a book review.
In the type community, we constantly say there is more to any given discussion of people than just personality type. What Will I Do With My Money? takes that admonition seriously. How we feel about money and what we do with it depends on more than type, and Linder spends the first section of his book, called The Meaning of Money, talking about those other factors. What are proud of in our handling of money, and what makes us ashamed? What motivates us in spending money - financial gain, comfort, or something else? What are our values? When we continually spend money in ways that are not in accord with our values, it causes stress. Linder talks about money memories, financial pathologies, and what contributes to self esteem. He doesn't mention personality until he gets to the five factors that affect our self esteem. Innate personality is one of them - the other four are family of origin, faith, peer group and society.
The second section is an introdution to personality type. In addition to the usual description of functions, types, and temperaments, he introduces his own terms for financial temperament. In relation to money, SJs are protectors, SPs are players, NFs are pleasers, and NTs are planners.
In the third and last section, Mind over Money, Linder goes over the implications of personality type, temperament and dominant function on the many issues related to money matters. He introduces his Golden Rule of personal finance: "Your goal in personal money management is to orient your life around the fulfillment of the material things that matter most." In a sense, the whole book is full of information and exercises to help you become aware of just how to do that it your own life.

Two Years Old
by Mary Hoerr, September 20, 2000

On September 16, 1998, this site debuted with my first and only two features week: It's Only Logical and Statistics. Since then, I've posted 96 feature articles, most of them written by me. Two years ago, I saw this site as a guide to web sites about Myers-Briggs topics, and that hasn't changed. However, in the meantime, internet jargon might have referred to this site variously as an ezine, a weblog, and most recently, a vortal (vertical portal).
A year ago, I was getting about 100 unique visitors a day, most of whom found me in the search engines, when they searched for Myers-Briggs. Today, I get 250 to 300 unique visitors a day, and about 60% still come through the search engines by searching on Myers Briggs or a four letter type. The top three search engines used to find this site were AOL.com search, MSN and Google, with Yahoo! coming in fourth. Last year, about 10 - 20% of you appeared to be regulars, and this year it looks like about 20%. An additional 20% of you followed links from another site.
New this year is the free subscription email newsletter of my This Week, which includes an archive of past issues. I have 300 subscribers, and my information about visitor types comes mostly from my subscriber poll. A year ago, most of you identified yourselves to me as preferring N in my site polls, but there was a sizable chunk of visitors that prefer S. This year's subscriber polling shows that the N preference outnumbers the S preference 6 to 1, and introverts outnumber extraverts 4 to 1. You are pretty evenly divided between the T and F preferences, and between the J and P preferences.
The format of the site hasn't changed much. I still provide four or five reviewed links every week, including one specifically relating to one of the sixteen types - my Focus on Type review. New this year is my top paragraph of This Week, which lets you know about new Net Link categories, non-profit conference, or other news. I've added a Frequently Asked Questions page and three new Net Link categories: nonEnglish resources, Online Communities and Careers. I still provide one original article every week, usually by me, but sometimes I get someone else to do it. Any more volunteers out there? 
Last year I added feedback forms all over the site. Sometimes an entire week's edition is based solely on the feedback forms you sent me. So keep them coming! Two of you have suggested some sort of MBTI related bulletin board associated with this site. I'm considering it.
Last year I added the Amazon associate links, and the income provided now pays for my ISP and site hosting fees (and a salary of about 10 cents an hour). I've been playing around with other associate programs, but so far I haven't hit on anything else that has worked as well (although I have high hopes for Make Your Site Sell). I'm still trying to find a way to make this site pay for the time and money I invest in it. Any suggestions are appreciated.
I know this site looks cluttered, and can be hard to navigate. Someday, I hope to publish the link reviews from my own database, so you can put together web pages of link reviews based on your search criteria.
 This year I've been taking on more web design work, so I will probably cut down the frequency of my editions from once a week to twice a month, or maybe just once a month. I do absolutely all the work to keep this site up, from researching the links I review, to writing or finding feature articles, to writing html and configuring forms. Each weekly edition takes me from 5 to 10 hours to put together.
As I move into my third year on the web, I look forward to hearing from you. As I said in my report last year, you know I listen, and your comments may lead me to make a change to the site, or inspire an article. 

Thanks for visiting! 
Mary Hoerr

A Personal Tribute to Susan Brock
by Judy Allen, posted on September 13, 2000

My note: Susan Brock died on May 4, 2000. See the link review of Susan's site at Net Links > companies.

When Jean Kummerow spoke at Susan's funeral she described her as having "...an intense vibrant curiosity, every second, every hour, every day - she was always asking probing questions, and always learning..." This is a description which most of us would cherish and Susan certainly earned.
I met Susan some eight or nine years ago, when she was running a master class on 'using type in communication', in Oxford (UK). I was taken by her enthusiasm professionalism and humour. One of the greatest things she taught me right away - both of us having an ENTP preference - was that it was OK to be myself in training. I simply had to watch her interact with the group. I had recently qualified in the MBTI and was using it for a dissertation, for my Masters degree in Adult Education. I was in the process of soaking up as much information about type as possible.
As many of you will know, Susan had been involved with the MBTI for almost the whole of her working life. She was the second president of APT and gained awards for her work with the MBTI. She believed passionately in using type in a practical way - but a way grounded in research. She combined an apparent lightness of touch - mainly through humour - with a rigorous approach to theory. When teaching she had antennae poised for every teaching point - whether it be to recognise and reinforce positive points made or point out examples of biased or negative use of type.
As I listened to Susan teaching her FLEX Talk model, I was immediately taken with the idea of converting it into health care. We communicated for some time and then I met her twice back in Oxford where she "grilled" me about my ideas. She would often demonstrate to groups "what interest looks like in a thinking type" and I certainly experienced that and respected her for it.
We decided to go the whole distance - to write a book about type and health care - and developed a proposal together. By now I knew that Susan had cancer and we talked about how she would feel, writing about the very illness from which she suffered. When we were offered a contract, she was very ill indeed and offered to withdraw. I asked if she would go ahead - me drafting and collecting data, while she edited. She agreed and this was ideal.
I learnt so much from Susan's edits and enjoyed seeing my work continuously improved. She was aware that, as two ENTPs, we could have the same blind spots. But she could spot me writing from my own type - as in, for example, "...you have described 'I' as being 'not E'...".
We of course had similar senses of humour. Whilst I continued to be sensitive to the 'her illness/our work' connection, I relaxed when I tentatively sent a piece of what I thought was amusing writing by a cancer sufferer. She characteristically noted on the margin "Judy, I am snorting with laughter reading this!"
At no time did Susan directly use her own illness in our work - she managed to keep them separate, in an amazing way. When we prepared to present our material, and write papers for conferences, her academic rigour surfaced once more. As I have written elsewhere, second best would not do (many times I may have wished it might!). Presenting with her was fun - and interesting - as she became so wrapped up in her piece that time became of no moment.
I had not considered how closely one works with someone when co-authoring. I shiver to think of what might have been and how fortunate we were that it worked so well. Susan's illness was always present but she rarely complained - she saw it as part of her life. As Jean Kummerow said: "she saw it as something to be learned about, managed and lived with".
When Susan developed further problems in spring of this year, the outlook was bleak. I was pleased that I could go and stay with her, and take early copies of the book over to her. On that visit we also ran a pilot training course for health care students. None of them knew she was ill, but she was having daily treatment at that time and died two and a half weeks later. She even talked to me, on the phone, about the way her carers communicated with her during her last days, as well as checking up on what I was doing!
Susan had always planned to live, and I am sure that is why she outlasted the most optimistic forecasts. She touched the lives of many who, like me, have reason to be grateful for her wisdom and generosity.
We argued constantly over who should be the first author - the publishers saying it was unheard of to have two authors both wanting the other to go first! In the end, she won - saying prophetically that I was the Mother, she the midwife.
Whilst I have written of my own experience I know many people could write similarly. In the wider sense our partnership reflects one of the many gifts of the MBTI. It introduced us in the first place and helped us to work productively together both personally and professionally in a way that for me was truly inspirational - and great fun.

Judy Allen ( allen@macklin.kc3ltd.co.uk ) is the co-author, with Susan Brock of "Health Care Communication using Personality Type - Patients are Different!" ( Routledge 2000)

ENTJ Leader
by Mary Hoerr, September 6, 2000

The last feedback question in July simply asked what was on your mind. I got only one response, but it was so interesting I thought I'd share it with you and get your help. The anonymous ENTJ writes:

I am totally stuck in my career. Something must be awry because although I come across quite typically for my type i.e. I am perceived as a natural leader with high persuasive skill (my way of being directive I think:)) and a great 'story teller' I have not yet been able to get a real position of leadership in the companies I work in. The 'real' leaders use me to rally the troops, troubleshoot stuff, sell and champion ideas but I want to be the 'real' leader with the money and title etc. I want to know where I am stuck and would like to use some understanding of my type and how it interacts with other types to help analyze the problem. Any suggestions?

This question brings up a delicate point. While it is good to focus on the positive attributes of each type, type theory also implies that each type will also have blind spots and weak areas. One site I have reviewed discusses how type can affect miscommunication. Check out my link review in Net Links > etc : Achilles Tendencies: Exploring Human Frailty and Personality Type. He mentions that one of the defensive Satir modes that may be more likely for TJ's is the blaming mode.
A very practical book that discusses the impact of type on leadership style, including weaknesses and blind spots is: Hardwired Leadership by Roger R. Pearman. For more info on the book, as well as a link to my book review, go to Net Links > books : Hardwired Leadership. There is a fascinating chart in the book that lists what leadership qualities each type sees in themselves, which of those qualities are also seen by observers, and which qualities are not seen by observers (blind spots). Leadership qualities not seen by observers of ENTJ's are "teamwork, developing relationships, being self-aware, being free of interpersonal difficulty, commitment to management's vision." The book also goes through communication styles, preconceptions, hot buttons and more for each type.
Finally, there's the usual caveat: type doesn't explain everything. There may be something totally unrelated to type that is keeping this ENTJ from the position he or she wants.

Take a Look at This Site
by Mary Hoerr, August 30, 2000

How Many People Visit This Site?
Starting in June 2000, MyComputer.com started keeping track of unique monthly visitors to this site (before that, I could see unique visitors per day - so someone who visited on several different days would be counted as unique for each day, but would show several times in a month). In June there were over 4700 unique visitors, in July, over 6700 unique visitors, in August so far there have been over 7300. Looks like the number of unique visitors is going up, even though the (northern hemisphere) summer months are supposed to be the slowest on the web. Unique visitors per day in August have ranged from a low of 166 to a high of 367. Increase in traffic has been consistent but not dramatic this year.

What Pages Are Most Popular?
Not suprisingly, this is the most visited page on this site. The most popular pages in August are: This Week, Tests, Types, Links, Features, 1999 Third Quarter Features Archive, Companies, Fun(ny), INFP, Careers, Research. The same list for the year would be: This Week, Tests, Types, Links, Features, Search, Companies, 1999 Third Quarter Features Archive, INFP, Fun(ny), About This Site, Research. The most visited individual type pages so far this year (go to Types) are INFP (1967 page views), ISTJ, INTJ, INTP, ENFP, INFJ, ENFJ, ESTJ, ENTP, ENTJ, ISFJ, ESFJ, ISTP, ISFP, ESFP and ESTP (307 page views).

Who Subscribes to the Newsletter?
There are 284 subscribers to the email newsletter version of this site. Although this site was first published on September 16, 1998, the newsletter didn't start until November 11, 1999. According to the current poll running among newsletter subscribers (63 responses or 22% response rate), the most common subscriber type is INTP, closely followed by INFP and INFJ. The most common "S" types are ISTJ and ISFJ. The NF temperament is most common, followed by the NT temperament and the SJ temperament, with the SP temperament being the lowest. No subscribers with the ISFP, ISTP and ESFJ types have participated in the poll. Subscribers are pretty evenly divided between T and F, with slightly more preferring F. Those with the N preference outnumber those with the S preference roughly 6 to 1. Introverts outnumber Extraverts roughly 4 to 1. There is a definite but small preference for P over J. So the "composite type" of newletter subscribers that have participated in the poll so far is INFP.

DESIGNATION OF TRADEMARK SYMBOLS

ALWAYS USE A TRADEMARK AS AN ADJECTIVE

Always use a trademark as an adjective, NEVER as a noun or a verb. The value of a trademark is that it associates a product with the owners of that products. Witch Houses
by Mary Hoerr, July 19, 2000 

Just recently, I joined the rest of the reading human race and read my first Harry Potter, fittingly enough, the first one - Harry Potter and the Sorcerer's Stone. The school Harry goes to, the Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry, divides the student body into four houses, so I couldn't resist trying to sort them out by temperament. All Hogwarts house descriptions are taken from Harry Potter and the Sorcerer's Stone, by J. K. Rowling (Buy it at Amazon). All temperament quotes are taken from Please Understand Me II, by David Keirsey. (* Buy it at Amazon * Read My Review * Link Review *)
Gryffindor is the house Harry and his friends end up in. Its symbol is the Lion. Dumbledore, the headmaster of the school was in this house, as well as Harry's father. Gryffindor is for the brave at heart, who have daring, nerve and chivalry.
I think Gryffindor corresponds to the SP or artisan temperament. According to Please Understand Me II, "Artisans see themselves, and wish to be seen by others, as bold, daring, venturesome - audacious."
Hufflepuff has the badger for its symbol. None of Harry's friends or enemies end up in this one. In conversations among the incoming first year students, the only comment anyone makes about Hufflepuff is that they don't want to end up there. Hufflepuff is for the just and the loyal, who are not afraid of hard work.
I think Hufflepuff corresponds to the SJ or guardian temperament. Guardians wish to "present themselves as dependable, trustworthy, or accountable in shouldering their responsibilities."
The next two houses, Ravenclaw and Slytherin, correspond to NF and NT, although I'm not completely convinced which is which.
Ravenclaw is considered by characters to be an acceptable second choice to Gryffindor by Harry's friend Hermione. Its symbol is the eagle. None of Harry's friends or enemies end up in this one. Ravenclaw is for the wise, who have wit and learning. This could fit the NT or rational temperament, which seeks for knowledge, or the NF temperament which aspires to be a sage.
Slytherin is the house where most, if not all, of Harry's enemies are (for students) or were (for adults). Its symbol is the snake. Slytherin is for the cunning, who will use any means to achieve their ends. The problem with determining the temperament represented by this house is that in the first book, at least, there are no positive examples from this house. In determining the temperament, I have to look at the negative aspects of the NF and NT temperaments. The traits shown by the enemies seem to me to be mostly power lust, and the NT thirst for competency, gone astray, could take that form, I think.
So my final list: Gryffindor - SP, Hufflepuff - SJ, Ravenclaw - NF, Slytherin - NT. Although the houses are sorted by temperament, I don't think that the temperament of each character matches the temperament I assign to the house. Dumbledore is from SP Gryffindor, but I'm pretty sure he's NT.

What Type is Sherlock Holmes?
by Mary Hoerr, reposted on July 12, 2000 Reprint from May 3, 1999 

I and T is given, I think. The question is: 
INTJ, ISTJ, INTP, ISTP? 

N vs S 
With Holmes' kind of deduction, you have to be constantly focused on all the details in your environment. This is extraverted S, not introverted S, where you are continually comparing the external data with your internal standards of perfection. 
Let's look at Ti, with extroverted perception, whether N or S. Extraverted N would focus on patterns as input, extraverted S would focus on the facts, as input. In either case, Ti is logical and impersonal but makes decisions "based on probabilities" and "leaves room for the unexpected." Introverted thinking coordinates our "behaviors logically with immediate sensory data" or in the case of Ne, with immediate patterns. (Quotes from Lenore Thomson's "Personality Type: An Owner's Manual" book. See book review at Features > 1999 First Quarter Features Archive > Personality Type: An Owners Manual Book Review. 
Thus, the ISTP athlete coordinates his behaviors logically with the factual details of where the ball is located. The INTP programmer changes her software program as she works on it as the patterns become more apparent. 
Kind of a litmus test to tell INTP from ISTP could be sports. ISTP's are naturals, INTP's have to work hard at it. Doesn't sound like Sherlock worked constantly on his boxing. Also, violin playing would fit in  more with ISTP's love of tools and instruments. 

P vs J
I think his dominant function was a judging one, and that his perception had to be extraverted to get the data he needed for his cases. 

Junkie
Remember in Sherlock's world, opium was not illegal. It was just like any other addictive vice: tobacco or alcohol. If Sherlock were "alive" today, he certainly wouldn't be a junkie, because of the loss of control caused by addiction to an illegal substance, especially since his passion was solving crimes. 
That's my take on it, anyway.

Lessons from the Window Seat Book Review

by Mary Hoerr, July 5, 2000  
Lessons from the Window Seat, Achieving Shared Vision in the Workplace by David Specht, forward by Linda Berens. Back on June 7 I did a link review referencing Specht's article on employee retention (see Net Links > business). He got in touch with me, and asked if he could send me this book. I agreed, and after reading it decided to do a book review.

This book is written as the story of a fictional manager who is no longer satisfied at work. Bob Sippel is frustrated by his situation at work after budget cuts at work leave him as the only remaining project manager in R & D. His wife asks him where he wants to be in the next five years and what he needs from his company. Bob reflects on the answers, notices concrete examples around him, and discusses them with colleagues. In the process, Bob discovers that before he can align his vision with his company's, he has to first know both his own vision and his company's.
Part of the story, of course, deals with type, or more accurately, temperament. Specht's method of helping you determine your temperament uses a somewhat different method than I've seen elsewhere. Instead of a list of questions, he asks you to read the needs and values for each temperament, circling those that are most important to you. Then at the bottom of the page, there are blank lines where you are to note down where in your life you meet these needs and values. You do this for each temperament. Presumably, you can then go back and identify your temperament by finding the temperament where you made the most circles. This also has the very positive side effect of stressing that whatever your temperament is, you still have aspects of the other temperaments.
By using the story format, Specht is able to provide gentle criticism of things that do not contribute to shared vision without pointing fingers. However, the examples are concrete enough (differing levels of customer service during Sippel's plane ride) to clearly illustrate his points.
Another interesting aspect of this book is that the protagonist is a Guardian (SJ) who has changed jobs several times. Another character is an Artisan (SP) who has never changed jobs at all. Although that seems to go against what we think of the SP and SJ temperaments, it points out that an SP who finds sufficient change and challenge on the job may stay in one job for a long time, while even an SJ may find himself changing jobs if he doesn't find one that meets his core needs.
