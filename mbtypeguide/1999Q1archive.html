Christian Doctrine and Type
by Mary Hoerr, March 29, 1999 

Type theory is a study of opposites. There are two opposite ways of gathering energy (E and I), two ways of perceiving (N and S), two ways of making judgments (F and T), and two preferred ways of dealing with the outer world (J and P). Neither way is better than the other: they are either preferred or not preferred, appropriate to a given situation or goal, or not appropriate. 
It appears from Rev. Hird's article that the philosophy from which Jung derived type theory went much farther than that. Jung apparently did not believe in the Christian idea that good and evil are opposite realities that are not merely different aspects of a higher reality. 
Myers-Briggs type theory is definitely derived in part from Jung's psychology. But it was also partially derived independently by Myers and Briggs observing the traits of schoolchildren. Niednagel also came up with the idea of Brain Types, which also uses the Myers-Briggs categories, by observing children playing sports. I don't think there is anything necessarily contrary to Christianity in applying Myers-Briggs type. 
Might one start with the Myers-Briggs type pairs, which are opposite but morally neutral, and be led on to the idea that good and evil are also opposite but morally neutral? Yes. And one might also go on to more Jungian theories that are not compatible with Christianity. Certain practitioners that follow new age or some eastern philosophies may very well see Myers-Briggs type as one aspect of their moral relativism. 
It may be that Christian counselors need to be very careful in their use of Myers-Briggs type, especially since I think we live in relativistic culture, at least in the United States. But I think it can also fit it with the Christian idea that every individual is given unique gifts by God that are different but equally valid. The title of "Gifts Differing" comes directly from a Bible quote of Paul, explaining that everyone's gifts were to be respected.
What do you think? Do you think Myers-Briggs type leads to moral relativism? Do you believe in moral relativism yourself? If you are Christian, have you found contradictions between Myers-Briggs type and your faith? Let me know by sending me your feedback. I'm always interested in your thoughts and opinions. 

The Unicorn Type - ISFJ
by Mary Hoerr, March 22, 1999 

My 5-year-old daughter has been very interested in unicorns lately. We have been watching the video The Last Unicorn (A Rankin/Bass Production in association with ITC Films, screenplay by Peter S. Beagle, based on his novel). It got me to thinking that unicorns could be a good symbol of the ISFJ type. 
"The unicorn is the only fabulous beast that does not seem to have been conceived out of human fears. In even the earliest references he is fierce yet good, selfless yet solitary, but always mysteriously beautiful. He could be captured only by unfair means, and his single horn was said to neutralize poison." -Marianna Mayer, The Unicorn and the Lake 
The author of The Unicorn's Meadow site writes: 
Today’s Unicorn is special to each of his followers, but he still holds the basic principles of nobility, selflessness, and purity. And he shall always be one of the most beautiful creatures conceived by the mind of Man.
In my article on SJ's, I found a desire for perfection (see The Search for Perfection ) as a motivation for SJ's. The unicorn, pure and beautiful, is a symbol of perfection. 
The unicorn protects the animals in her woods. In The Last Unicorn, the unicorn goes on a quest to save the other unicorns who are trapped  in sea. It seems not to be a glorious quest but a heavy burden and a duty. 
Keirsey describes the ISFJ as The Protector. "The primary desire of the Protector is to be of service and to minister to others, but here "service" means not so much providing others with the necessities of life (the Provider's concern) as protecting others from life's pitfalls and injuries." 
It may seem ironic, to pick a fantasy animal as the image of the ISFJ. But on reflection, what real life creature could model protection of others, with perfect purity and beauty? 

What do you think? Let me know by sending me your feedback. I'm always interested in your thoughts and opinions. 

Your Key to Sports Success Book Review

by Mary Hoerr, March 15, 1999 

It is rare to find a book on type that appears to be written from the perspective of the S preference. Your Key to Sports Success, by Jonathon P. Niednagel, is such a book. Although he never identifies his type, except for the T preference, in the book, the book seems clearly targeted toward the S preference. 
Page after page of the book is filled with information on how the playing styles of various athletes reflect and is influenced by their type. And there is a clear and practical point to all of this: Niednagel provides specific guidance to athletes to help them improve their performance based on type. I had trouble with the style of the book, which seemed to drown in facts about athletes, except for an interesting chapter on brain functioning (which Niednagel considered "optional"). Because of this, my first impression was that the book was superficial. However, recognizing my own N bias, I realized I was connecting "theory" with "important" and "facts" with "trivial". After that, I was able to appreciate the book on its own terms. 
Niednagel does not like the term personality type, preferring instead to relate the Myers-Briggs types to brain "wiring". Thus, he refers to "Brain Types®", a term he has registered, and he calls his web site The Brain Type® Institute. He says that the personality of an individual is dependent on many other factors besides their Brain Types®. Here is one example he gives: "Baseball's Steve Carlton and Pete Rose, both retired, are ISTPs. Steve was widely known in his playing days for his laconic style and refused to talk to the press; whereas Pete became known for his gregariousness." Another example from tennis: John McEnroe and Stefan Edberg are both ISTPs. 
He makes an interesting case that the different function pairs can be related to different physical skills. Thus, the ST excels at fine motor skills, the SF at gross motor skills, the NF at "body harmony", and the NT at ... well, nothing physical. But we can become great athletes because if an NT claims something within her sphere of capability, she'll work day and night until she's one of the best. A corollary of this is that by watching how someone plays a sport, Niednagel claims to be able to tell the person's type. 
Another plus for the book is the one-word descriptions he gives for each type. For example, instead of the usual (boring, to me) characterization as "inspector", or "fulfilling duty" descriptions, he calls the ISTJ an "investigator." And he calls the INTP a "logician" rather than an "architect". While logician is correct for INTP as far as it goes, I think architect is a much better term. It makes me wonder whether there are similar "correct as far as it goes" but not really fitting terms being used for other types, especially in the SJ and SP descriptions, since most type descriptions I come across are written by NTs or NFs. 
This is a good introduction to the usefulness of type for people with an S preference - or who just love sports! 
Have you ever thought of different one-word descriptors for the SP or SJ types? Let me know by sending me your feedback. I'm always interested in your thoughts and opinions. 

Inside my Head: INTP and INFP
by Mary Hoerr, March 8, 1999 

In my article The Search for Perfection a couple of weeks ago, I described what I imagined the inner world of the SJ, especially those with introverted Sensing dominant (ISFJ and ISTJ) looked like. This week I'm going to try the same thing on the two intuitive types who have a dominant introverted judging function: INTP and INFP. 
Because I am introvert, I "live" in my inner world, not in the external world. This should also apply to the INFP. 
Because my dominant function is a judging function, I organize where I live - that is, my inner world. This should also apply to the INFP. 
Because my judging function is T, I organize according to objective truth, which I subjectively believe in. The INFP would organize according value judgments and feeling. 
My auxiliary function is iNtuition, which is extroverted. External information acts as a trigger to my inner organizing. I don't notice my surroundings at all, except as a point of departure for current interests. In fact, it sometimes seems that the greatest indicator of my iNtuitive preference is my lack of Sensing observation. I miss my exit, I don't notice the large birthday balloons festooning a coworker's office, and so on. Most of the time, when I am not forced to interact with the outside world, I am organizing my thoughts on some subject. One current interest is an attempt to depict to myself the inner and outer worlds of each temperament and type. I imagine that an INFP would be organizing how she values and feels about things. 
I picture the inner world of my mind as a relational database. Data is collected, but only retained when it can be logically connected with pieces of data. One connection only - name with person, for example - is not good for remembering. Multiple connections are better. 
I picture the inner world of an INFP mind as a spider web. As in my case, there are many connections, going in many directions. But the connections are not defined logically, but by "feel", just as one can imagine "feeling" and "pulling on" spider web strands of different lengths and thicknesses. 
In both cases, the N preference implies multiple connections among many pieces of information. The dominant T preference implies logically defined connections, manipulated by changing formulae. The dominant F preference implies elastic, "felt" connections which are "moved" and "pulled" to change them. Somehow the NT structure seems "square" to me and the NF structure seems "oval". 
Am I all wet? I know a lot of you are INFPs and INTPs. Does any of this sound like you? Let me know by sending me your feedback. I'm always interested in your thoughts and opinions. 

Attention Deficit Disorder and Temperament
Guest Feature by Teresa Gallagher, March 1, 1999, website Born to Explore 
Copyright © 1999, Teresa Gallagher

Thanks for reviewing my site, [link review: Net Links > teaching: Attention Deficit Disorder, NP's and SP's] but I think you've misunderstood my thoughts on the subject. I don't think Attention Deficity Disorder (ADD) is an "N" thing, nor do I think N's are more creative in general than the SPs (I do think they're more creative than the SJs, though). I think of creativity as more of a "P" thing. Actually, I tend to think of ISFPs as ultimately being the most creative in an artistic sense. But the SPs rarely get on the internet, and that's why they don't show up on my site. 
I do think that SPs are more likely to have the standard hyperactive/problem behavior in school, but that they are also more likely to find occupations which meet their experiential needs. NPs are more likely to do OK academically, but underachieve and often feel "different", spacey and sometimes pretty hyper (ENFP, ENTP). As adults, it seems that NPs may have more problems finding a job they are happy with for more than a year or two, partly because most jobs are very concrete and NPs aren't. I do suspect that a proportionately higher percentage of adults going in for ADD diagnosis are NPs. And I disagree with Keirsey in his belief that ADD is strictly an SP thing; I think it probably used to be, but since Keirsey wrote "Please Understand Me" ADD has changed from a focus on hyperactivity to a focus on inattention, away from problem behavior and towards underachievers. This doesn't mean I think it's strictly an NP thing though, either. For one thing, there aren't very many NPs compared to SPs. 
My focus on MBTI is not so much to say that certain types are ADDish, but to say that there is no one ADD temperament, and to introduce my readers to the concept of temperament variation. The ESTP classified as ADD/hyperactive is very different from the INFP classified as ADD/inattentive. When I read ADD case studies, the various MBTI types tend to jump out. For most ADDers, I think the MBTI descriptions more often describe them better than the ADD descriptions, and in a much more positive, natural way. The only reason I talk a lot about INFPs, INTPs, ENFPs and ENTPs is because the vast majority of my readers are those temperaments and most people want to read about themselves, after all.

The Search for Perfection
by Mary Hoerr, February 22, 1999 

I have often felt that there was something missing in the description of the SJ temperament. While it seemed correct, as far as it went, describing the SJ goal as "belonging" seemed off to me - as if someone said the NT goal was "reading books." Certainly, there has probably never been an NT who didn't like to read, but the love of reading follows from the "need" to know as much as possible and gather competencies or powers. I could also see how that could follow from the combination of the N and T functions. 
But how do you get "needs to belong" from SJ? I think an important clue is that all SJ's share introverted sensing as the dominant or auxiliary function. In fact, for the rest of this article, I'll concentrate just on the two SJ's that have introverted sensing as their dominant function. 
We think of Sensors as realistic, and they are. We especially think of SJ's as matter-of-fact, detail oriented, pillars of society. So when I read Binnie Ferrand, an ISFJ, in Some Observations of an Introverted Dominant Sensor, I was surprised to find her saying: 

Introverted sensation, when it is differentiated, is highly tuned and spiritual, not being limited to the actual physical sensation. It is perhaps the most inarticulate function of all and is best expressed in color or form.

The introverted sensor experiences her inner world of concrete facts as "color" or "form." 

Then, I read Joe Butt's functional analysis of introverted sensing (from ISTJ ): 

A "proper" chair has four legs, (and other qualities this poor INTP can only guess). Jung viewed introverted sensing as something of an oxymoron, in that the natural direction of senses is outward toward the object, rather than inward and away from it. One has the sense that Introverted Sensors are drawn more to the measure of the concept of the perceived object than to the experience of that perception.

Not only are the sensory data experienced as form, but as "proper" form. 
The final piece fell into place when I read John Fudjack's description of the "enlightened" quality of Enneagram Type One, which he correlates to all the types that end in J. Usually called the Perfectionist, he describes the positive quality of Enneagram Type One as a search for perfection and completeness. 

Using Maslow's convention, we might distinguish between B-perfection (or B-completion) and a degenerate form that we might call 'D-perfection'. D-perfection, then, would be the need to be right, in perfect order, finished. 

Could the dominant goal of the ISTJ and ISFJ be "perfection" and "completeness"? I imagine that the SJ gathers sensory data to create a concrete, perfect form. In interacting with the world, the SJ continually gathers more sensory data to add to the internal "perfect form" and at the same time, maps the internal perfect form onto the outer world. The goal is to have both the internal form and the outer reality match. Since the SJ's forms are based on concrete data, the internal and the external can actually be made to match. 
This would have certain implications. It would make the SJ disposed toward keeping things organized and in good order (logistics) because the SJ naturally looks at objects in the real world and is instantly aware of the difference between that object and its ideal state. To see something is to know whether and how it needs to be maintained. 
This would also explain the satisfaction that the SJ finds in doing routine tasks. In such tasks, the inner ideal and the outer reality can be made to match regularly. This "matching" is a source of pleasure, I imagine, much as the discovery of a new logical connection can be for an NT. 
Finally, this would also imply the SJ love of tradition and belonging. Through tradition and belonging to social groups, the SJ has access to a rich deposit of already developed inner forms, painstakingly built up of an immense amount of concrete data. Tradition, to an SJ, may be the NT equivalent of a library. 
What do you think about SJ's and the search for perfection? Let me know by sending me your feedback. I'm always interested in your thoughts and opinions.

The ST Double Shift
by Mary Hoerr, February 15, 1999 

Corey, editor of the Epub Dissension, (no longer available on the internet as of 24 May 2000) has made some Observations. Based on people he knows of different types, he concludes that the ISTJ type seems to have the most resistence to type theory. Since Corey's sample is admittedly small, I wondered whether his observation may be applicable to ST types in general, especially those who also have a J attitude. 
This occurred to me because I thought of my own reactions and experiences to type. The social norm (most common type functions) are ST. As an INTP, then, I shared one function (T) with the social norm. On first learning about type, my first reaction was that it described me well. But the next reaction, occurring at various levels over many years, was the flip side of that realization: that there were other ways of judging and perceiving that were just as normal and valid as mine. 
Of course, this was no news when I thought of the perceiving function. Since S is the norm, I could hardly get through life without being aware of the existence and utility of the sensing function. However, the judging function was a different story. 
As a T, I fit the norm, which meant that I had less reason to be aware of or value the F function. As a woman, perhaps, I was not quite as unaware of it as a man might have been (women being more socialized to use F), but I still equated F judgement with emotionalism. 
As I came to see the validity of F judgement, I found myself starting to second-guess my own judgements, now that I was aware of a factor that I had been mising. I also found myself re-evaluating my opinion of other people's judgements. 
For an ST, this uncomfortable process of realizing that you have been missing something important would happen for both the perceiving and the judging preferences. 
That may also explain why the NF temperament seems to take to the theory so easily. In our ST culture, they are already quite aware of S and T. Myers-Briggs goes on to tell them that their own preferences of N and F are also valid. 
What do think about how the different types or temperaments see type? Let me know by sending me your feedback. I'm always interested in your thoughts and opinions.

ISTJ's and Careers
by Mary Hoerr, February 1, 1999 

It's no surprise that the most common type in an informal survey of Mechanical Design classes are NT's, with INTP being most common, and ENTP being third most common. More interesting is that the second most common type is ISTJ. 
Looking back at the career listing by the Department of the Interior, in my Research Net Links, the job closest to Mechanical Design listed for the ISTJ was computer programmer. There were no listings in the area of science (except geologist), engineering, or design. 
Wondering about what other information I could find on research dealing with ISTJ job preferences, I went back to Damian Conway's The Careers and Personality Page, also in my Research Net Links, where people who know their type enter how satisfied they were with various types of jobs. I searched on all ISTJ's and got 18 entries (out of a database of 230 entries). Of these entries, the three jobs that were listed as extremely satisfying, the highest rating, were: Counselor, Firefighter, and Process Engineer. 
The Counselor entry surprised me, but one problem I have with many of these job groupings by type is that they seem to be based on theory rather than statistics. Are there any ISTJ's among my readers who would like to set me straight? What is attractive about being a counselor to you? How accurate do you think ISTJ career lists are?

Talking about Type
by Mary Hoerr, January 25, 1999 

The Institute for Type Development (ITD), an Australian leading provider of Training and resources for Psychological Type, Personality Type, Myers-Briggs Type Indicator® (MBTI®) instrument and Temperament Theory, publishes an on-line newsletter, and the August 1998 newsletter included the newest language guidelines for MBTI® practitioners. I'm going to study them and make sure I follow their guidelines for the most part, although I'm not an MBTI® practitioner. A very important point: "One must always express terms in such a way as to convey the concept of preferences and not hard - and fast categories. ... correct - Feeling types, incorrect - Feelers." It may seem a minor difference, but Feeling types describes one aspect of a person, Feeler makes it sound like the only important aspect of the individual is their Feeling preference. 
I also like their guideline to "use the phrase 'clarity of preference' instead of 'strength of preference'." Using the term "strength" implies to me that it is better to have a stronger preference than a weak preference. There is less of a value judgement implied with "clarity of preference." 
One other guideline I don't like as much. "Do not use a single letter in text to abbreviate a preference." I'm sure that this guideline is provided in the interest of cutting down on jargon and making talk about preferences easier to follow. Those of you who've been reading my reviews and features know that I tend to use single letters as much or more often than writing out preferences. I have a reason I do this. 
One of my big problems with the terms used by the MBTI® inventory to denote the preferences is that they have a lot connotations associated with them that are not implied by the theory. The I-E dichotomy is probably not so affected by this, but Feeling, Thinking, Sensing, Intuiting, Judging and Perceiving have strong associations. Although a Thinking preference does not imply no feelings, and a Feeling preference does not imply inability to think clearly, use of the words leads to that unintended bias. 
In Please Understand Me II, Keirsey gets around some of those problems by using different descriptive words for each preference. His terms are:

This seems to me to be a step in the right direction, but I still think the least possibility for false associations is just to use the letters alone. 
How else could we use the preference terms in less misleading ways? Let me know by sending me your feedback. I'm always interested in your thoughts and opinions. 

Going Against Type
by Mary Hoerr, January 18, 1999 

There are many resources that describe the jobs that seem best suited to each type, or that each type actually tends to go into. See Net Links > Research for past site reviews on that subject. So why would you pick a job that wasn't suited to your type? 
Besides the obvious answer that type doesn't determine everything, and that no correlation between type and job satisfaction is going to be perfect, I think there are other reasons. As Otto Kroeger and Janet Thuesen point out in their book, Type Talk, any organization that is too concentrated in certain types is going to have blind spots. They use the example of a group of 6 dentists, all ISTJ, who went into business together. The business wasn't doing well, because, among other things, they weren't making people feel good about their practice. So although the ISTJ type might seem to be well suited to dentistry, perhaps a type with an F preference could make a strong contribution. 
I can also think of the example of a home based accountant I know. I don't know her type, but she doesn't look much like the "typical" accountant. The best way I know to describe how she dresses is "artistically". That is, sometimes she's in fashion, and sometimes she's conservative, but both considerations seem to be rather beside the point. She is outgoing and friendly, but not afraid to tell you what she thinks. Would you let an accountant like her do your books? Well, evidently, to many people her personal style inspires trust. People like her, talk to her, and tell her the things accountants need to know. And she has more business than she can handle, to the point where she gets to pick and choose her clients. 
On the other hand, in their excellent book,  The Challenge of Change in Organizations: Helping Employees Thrive in the New Frontier, Nancy Barger and Linda Kirby use the excellent example of an I-SJ manager who comes up with a very effective plan for dealing with changes in her organization. 
I like the way Linda Tobey, life coach, seems to deal with type. She uses type to help in communicating with the people she coaches. In one of her examples, the coach doesn't tell Bob, an ISTJ engineer to forget about his promotion to Project Manager because he lacks the "F" people skills. And the coach also doesn't just say: you've got to be more friendly and interested in people. Instead, his coach "designed structures to support Bob that are chunked-down and actionable, logical, concrete, and experiential."
Can you think of other examples where someone who would seem to be the "wrong type" for the job did an exceptional job? Let me know by sending me your feedback. I'm always interested in your thoughts and opinions. 

Concrete Imagination
by Mary Hoerr, January 11, 1999 

Because most people are sensors, the kind of perceiving that intuitives do often gets called "imagination." If the imagination leads to practical results, it seems like magic. Otherwise, it's just flaky. What sensors do is just considered normal, or common sense. 
But if imagination means to create something that did not exist before, it is clear that imagination can be concrete or abstract. Using iNtuition, a person may come up with an abstract idea called a dragon, described vaguely as a large, fire breathing serpent with bat wings. But to bring the abstract idea of "dragon" to life requires Sensing, to fill in the details. And filling in the details requires imagination. 
I notice that my 5-year-old daughter, who I suspect prefers sensing, does not seem to create any new creatures. But she can "play" with a pony, or a dog, or other animal that is not really there. And her "playmate" has very detailed characteristics. The pony is not just "Rosie", for example, but a pony with a certain color mane, tail, bridle, eyes, legs, and special markings. And she can draw a detailed picture of the pony. 
Concrete imagination creates an individual concrete example of a class that already exists. From object oriented programming, our N gives us the class, and our S instantiates it. Our N creates the abstract class called "pony", and our S gives us an individual pony, with a name and concrete characteristics. 
Another aspect of concrete imagination is the ability to imagine how an object will look (or be sensed) under different circumstances. Thus, we use sensing to imagine how that dress on the rack will look when we put it on, or how the addition of a different spice, or more or less of the same spice, will affect a recipe we are using. Concrete imagination tells an artist how to mix his colors, or a jazz player how certain notes will sound in a jam session. 
I see my daughter do this when she makes things. Sometimes she uses building blocks, but she seems to be able take anything at hand to create what she has in mind. She does not create something new, but uses the materials around her to create something she knows. Thus, she made a "computer" out of two plastic kiddie chairs, cardboard, and string. She made a boat out of a box, with a toy drum stick for a paddle. And then, before she went to do something else, she created something to keep the paddle from getting lost (when she went canoeing, she was told to be sure not to lose the paddle) out of wooden blocks and string. 
When we call imagination an N characteristic, we run the risk of mis-typing many people with a Sensing preference. Can you think of other examples of concrete vs abstract imagination? Or do you think I'm wrong, or have another perspective? Let me know by sending me your feedback. I'm always interested in your thoughts and opinions. 

Please Understand Me II Book Review

by Mary Hoerr, January 4, 1999 

Since David Keirsey's first version of this book, Please Understand Me, was the first type book I read (see About This Site), I was anxious to read his updated version when it came out this year. I finally got a copy of Please Understand Me II for Christmas, and it's not just a minor revision - there is lots of new material in it. 
He has re-named the four temperament types to use Plato's terms, instead of Greek gods. The SP's are now the Artisans, the SJ's the Guardians, the NF's are Idealists, and the NT's are Rationals. Each temperament is divided into two roles, and then each four-letter type has a descriptive name. As an INTP, my temperament is Rational, my role is Engineer, and the unique name for my type is Architect. More detailed descriptions are available at Keirsey's site. 
Why the emphasis on descriptive terms rather than function letters? Keirsey's view of type takes a different perspective than Myers and many other type theorists. To show the difference in perspective, I will quote Keirsey in his new book, p 31: 

"... personality, like anatomy, comes about not by an integration of elements, but by differentiation within an already integrated whole, emerging gradually as an individuated configuration. I claim an organism never becomes integrated because it is always integrated. It differentiates by a process of evolution into the mature form it is meant to become."

Thus, a person is not so much a collection of independent functions called INTP as an integrated type called an Architect, who, as she matures, will come more and more to possess a certain set of characteristics common to her type. 
One thing I particularly liked about the book was his new "matrix of character." Determining type or temperament through questionnaires and observation requires trying to determine mental viewpoints that are not observable. So Keirsey has come up with an extremely simple matrix of two easily observable characteristics to help determine temperament. People tend to be either concrete or abstract in their word usage. And in getting things done (using tools) they tend to put the priority either on cooperating with others or on the best way to use the tools - they are more cooperative or utilitarian. Thus, the SP is a concrete utilitarian, the SJ is a concrete cooperator, the NF is an abstract cooperator, and the NT is an abstract utiliarian. These do seem to me to be the most easily observable set of traits I've read about in the literature for a fast take on temperament. 
Keirsey describes each temperament and each individual type in terms of many different characteristics. He describes the kind of intelligence each temperament develops (Artisan-tactical, Guardian-logistical, Idealist-diplomatic, Rational-strategic), their interests, self-image and values. 
He also discusses something I hadn't come across before which he calls "orientation" (he doesn't mean the I-E orientation here). One's orientation is one's perspective on: the present, the future, the past, place and time. Especially the categories of place and time were interesting. It will come as no surprise that for Artisans the place is "here" and the time is "now". But the descriptions for the other temperaments, while seeming equally fitting, may bring a perspective you hadn't thought of before. 
There is a lot of new material in this book. It is not just a re-hashing of the first book. Keirsey has many new and interesting perspectives on type. If you've read this book, I would be interested in getting your feedback. What did you think of it? I'm always interested in your thoughts and opinions.
