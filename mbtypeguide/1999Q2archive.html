SpaceColonyH Game and Decision-Making
by Dennis Archambault, June 21, 1999 
copyright 1999 by Dennis Archambault 
Game Developer-Writer 
SpaceColonyH” 
Schgame@pacbell.net 

Mary's note: I reviewed this free game at Net Links > fun(ny). 

Visitors to Internet sites often have a variety of interests. I bet you are no exception. Most of you will probably be visiting this site for the first time, probably the vast majority, and the rest of you will have found it because of a focused search. Some of you may never return again, and others will be delighted again and again. Such is the world of the Internet. 
As the creator of the hyperlinked site (www.spacecolony.org or .com) to this webpage, I have been asked to write this brief article about the free download game, SpaceColonyH”. 
The game is a psychologically-based, problem-solving leadership game set in space. It is non-violent, non-racist and non-sexist. 
Decisions and decision making are the focus for what follows in this article, especially as it relates to personality: yours and mine. You can tell a lot about personalities by watching gestures. Definitive or soft, stabbing or encompassing, gestures reveal the inner world of people. They express themselves without being consciously aware of doing it. Really watch people… but then you probably already are if you are visiting this site. 
Hippocrates, the father of modern medicine, 2500 years ago categorized people into four main groups. Undoubtedly the names: Choleric, Sanguine, Melancholic and Phlegmatic mean little to you. Therefore, these names/categories have been modernized to be, respectively, the: Formal Leader, Informal Leader, Planner and Peacemaker. Fifteen people, eight women and seven men, were photographed gesturing in the four gestures representing the four personality types as described in my site. You will need look closely in order to ‘name’ the personality in each game turn. 
Decisions also have other influences: time and resources, being but two. When time is short, urgent perhaps two ‘decisive’ types will emerge and are needed: Formal Leaders and Informal Leaders. When circumspection and consensus building are suitable: Planners and Peacemakers come to the fore. Either set of two is appropriate when the timeliness for their types of decision is of greatest influence. 
Resources drive decisions as well. If supplies run short, do you take from others so that ‘production’ can continue unabated? Or do you ensure that everyone’s supply is protected no matter the consequences? If more people are needed for an urgent task, do you require people to put in heavy overtime, with little or no compensation, or do you 
conclude that a ‘lesser resolution’ is acceptable? 
These then are some of the considerations of the game: SpaceColonyH”. Each game has nine turns where the personality of the problem-reporting colonist has to be chosen and an operational decision has to be made. If you choose well, you will be ‘voted’ as Permanent Executive Director; if not, you are invited to try again. In fact, since the game is free, you are welcome to play again and again. There are almost 6000 game players worldwide. 
I will soon contract with my programmer to develop the mechanics of a review page where your answers will be compared with the ‘most correct choices’. So, try the game now and stay tuned to this new page as it gets added. 
Good choices to you.

In a Bookstore - INTP and ENTP 
by Andrew Mariano, June 7, 1999 
copyright 1999 by Andrew Mariano 

Mary's note: Andrew Mariano uses this bookstore scenario to illustrate the difference between  an ENTP and INTP. The ENTP wants to leave open the possibility of conversation with the person in the bookstore, because it might give him more ideas. The INTP doesn't want to be bothered by additional input while he's working out the inner logic of the book he's reading. 

Let's say you are in a bookstore, o.k.? Let's then say you are in the Sciences section. Are you still with me?  O.k.!  You're in a bookstore, you are browsing the science section, you're leafing through a book. At some point, another "person" wanders into the very same section as you, and it's a very small, cramped section. O.k. 
Let's begin with the assumption that you are an ENTP.  The other "person" ambles into your, heretofore exclusively occupied, space. You glance up from your leafing, look towards the other person.  (This is where it gets a liiiiittle dicey.) As an ENTP, you look towards their eyes. If they are looking for your eyes, you make brief eye contact, nod your head (only slightly), and make a feeble attempt at a smile (no teeth). Then, you make a gesture of movement (slight) to acquiesce a little of your space to the newcomer and say something like "Hi!"; "How are you?"; then go back to the leafing. 
However, if the other person makes no attempt at eye contact, you keep leafing and make no further gestures nor acquiesce any space until such time as the newcomer makes it obvious that they need to "get at" the space in your immediate vicinity. Then, you make another attempt at eye contact. If they engage, you send out a small talk salvo such as, "Excuse me."; or "Pardon me."; or "Are you trying to get here?"; while making a slight movement to give ground. If this person responds in kind (i.e. "Thank you."; "Pardon ME!"; . . . ) you make another innocuous comment that could potentially start a conversation, but, doesn't sound like a come on if the other person does not take the next step. Depending, of course, on the reaction of the other person, it is very likely that you will engage in some type of discussion with that person. 
O.K.?  So, now you're wearing an INTP hat, and the same scenario occurs. As soon as the other "person" enters the outer periphery of your comfort zone (about 10 to 11 feet) you move, as if you had already made the decision 5 minutes prior, to a new location and quickly set up a new comfort zone. 

How do other people, or the type literature in general, misunderstand what Extraverting or Introverting mean to you? (ie, E's talk all the time, I's are shy).

Murder Yet to Come Book Review
by Mary Hoerr, May 31, 1999 

Murder Yet to Come, by Isabel Briggs Myers (yes, she of Myers-Briggs Type Indicator® fame) is a mystery that won a contest over an Ellery Queen entry. It was so good I almost wished she'd stuck to mysteries instead of the type indicator. 

Jerningham, a playwright who writes and produces hit mystery plays, tries to figure out who murdered a millionaire, and the terrible secret of the millionaire's grand-niece, that she would rather die than tell. He is helped by two friends: his faithful secretary Mac, and his old buddy Carl Nilsson from Jerningham's time with Marines, who is now a homicide detective. There are no "tricks" in this mystery. If you are observant, you can figure out the mystery yourself, although I didn't. I was surprised at the ending, but it was the only ending that fit. In fact, I immediately reread the book, knowing how the murder was done and who did it, to see how it all fit together. 
The plot is clever, and the characters ring true. If you like mysteries, read this book. If you're just interested in type, you might want to read it just to figure out the types of the characters. Check it out from the library if you can find it, or if you want to buy it online, go to Amazon.com from here and I get a percentage.

A Hypothetical ISTJ Talks about Planning and Remembering
by Mary Hoerr, May 24, 1999 

In a previous articles, I have described how I saw my own INTP type (Inside my Head: INTP and INFP) and the ISFJ type (The Unicorn Type - ISFJ). In this article, I'll describe how I see the ISTJ type. Like the ISFJ, the dominant function of an ISTJ is introverted sensing. The auxiliary function is extraverted thinking. For some reason, I think this description works best in the first person. 
=====================
I live in my inner world, which is detailed, with color, sound, taste, touch and smell. What I remember I can bring up in full detail, like virtual reality with taste and smell thrown in. In fact, with more detail than virtual reality. 
I use objective logic when I have to leave my inner world and deal with the outside world. I look something over, determine what category it is in, and how it relates to my goals, and then, if appropriate, let it into my inner world. A lot of the time, I just use judgement to shorten my interaction with the outside world so I can go back to my inner world. 
People think I notice everything. When I look at a thing in the real world, I can instantly compare it with the many detailed versions I am constantly around in my inner world. That's why it's so easy for me to instantly spot anything different or out of the ordinary - or something that hasn't been cleaned or maintained correctly. I'm not necessarily looking for an error: it's almost like I can superimpose the thing I'm looking at over my inner image and see the differences. 
When I want to remember something, such as the name of a restaurant, I automatically add it to my inner world. I determine the details that are logically related to the restaurant, and add them. That means I don't just add the name of the restaurant, but its location, the kind of food they serve there, maybe the owner or maitre d', the part of town it's in, or whatever details I think are relevant. Once I've built up a complete picture of the restaurant in my inner world, it's almost impossible for me to lose it. 
Sometimes I irritate people because you can't just tell me the "name" of a restaurant. I want to know a lot more than that. As I've gotten older, I've learned to judge when to ask people for all the gritty details, and when to just look it up for myself. The result of this is that I never forget something if I've actually been given the time to plan it. Once I plan it, it's part of me. I could no more forget it than my head.
=====================

by Mary Hoerr, May 17, 1999 

Is there any relationship between type, and whether you are a night owl (most energetic late in the day) or a lark (a morning person)? I got an email request to check this out, and I've come up with a blank as far as web resources are concerned. That's hard to believe! So I'll have to give you my own thoughts, and ask for your feedback. 
I vs E: 
I think of E's as staying up later, because they don't want to miss anything going on when people are around. Might that translate to getting up later too? 
N vs S: 
I can't think of anything that would incline an N or an S more toward morning or evening.
T vs F: 
I can't think of anything that would incline T or an F more toward morning or evening. 
J vs P: 
I would guess that "J" types would most likely be morning persons, so that they could get the day to a good start. It's generally important for them to feel they have made specific accomplishments every day. Get up early, plan the day, execute the plan. 
My very unscientific analysis: 
Night owls: ENFP, ESFP, ENTP, ESTP 
Larks: INFJ, ISFJ, INTJ, ISTJ 
Either way: ENFJ, ESFJ, ENTJ, ESTJ, INFP, ISFP, INTP, ISTP 
So folks, please help me. Provide me your type and whether you are a night owl or a lark. And if you know of any web resources that link type and sleep patterns, let me know! We'll start with me: I'm a Night Owl.

ISTJs and Groups
by Mary Hoerr, May 10, 1999 

I got this email from an ISTJ who would like some feedback: 

Before [I found out I was an ISTJ] I was a number one [on the Enneagram]. I have been studying the enneagram for more than three years now. Just recently I went back to college, and learned of  "Myers and Briggs," and I can tell you, I have been fighting perfectionism my whole life. With the help of the enneagram, and more than likely age, I am finally learning to calm down. But, one thing puzzles me about my type, that makes me sometimes wonder if I am a ISTJ at all is, I do not like being like everybody else, I go out of my way to be different. Also, I really do not care about belonging to any group, I am mostly a loner. Also, I obsess on anything that catches my attention, I have to know everything there is to know about the subject. If you have any insight into this, it would be appreciated. Maybe you know of other ISTJs that are like this also. 

This made me think of  some ISTJ descriptions from Lenore Thomson's book, Personality Type: An Owner's Manual (see my book review: Features > 1999 Second Quarter Features Archive > Personality Type: An Owners Manual Book Review): 
Although ISTJs are indeed careful, and concerned to preserve what has been proved to be worthwhile, these characteristics are only a part of the type's approach to the world - the part that most people see. ISTJs are fundamentally Introverted Sensates, with a highly subjective, original turn of mind. 
... 
The very selectivity of the type's sensations gives ISTJs an extraordinary capacity for detail in the areas that strike them as important.

What Type is Sherlock Holmes?
by Mary Hoerr, May 3, 1999 

I and T is given, I think. The question is: 
INTJ, ISTJ, INTP, ISTP? 

N vs S 
With Holmes' kind of deduction, you have to be constantly focused on all the details in your environment. This is extraverted S, not introverted S, where you are continually comparing the external data with your internal standards of perfection. 
Let's look at Ti, with extroverted perception, whether N or S. Extraverted N would focus on patterns as input, extraverted S would focus on the facts, as input. In either case, Ti is logical and impersonal but makes decisions "based on probabilities" and "leaves room for the unexpected." Introverted thinking coordinates our "behaviors logically with immediate sensory data" or in the case of Ne, with immediate patterns. (Quotes from Lenore Thomson's "Personality Type: An Owner's Manual" book. See book review at Features > 1999 First Quarter Features Archive > Personality Type: An Owners Manual Book Review. 
Thus, the ISTP athlete coordinates his behaviors logically with the factual details of where the ball is located. The INTP programmer changes her software program as she works on it as the patterns become more apparent. 
Kind of a litmus test to tell INTP from ISTP could be sports. ISTP's are naturals, INTP's have to work hard at it. Doesn't sound like Sherlock worked constantly on his boxing. Also, violin playing would fit in  more with ISTP's love of tools and instruments. 

P vs J
I think his dominant function was a judging one, and that his perception had to be extraverted to get the data he needed for his cases. 

Junkie
Remember in Sherlock's world, opium was not illegal. It was just like any other addictive vice: tobacco or alcohol. If Sherlock were "alive" today, he certainly wouldn't be a junkie, because of the loss of control caused by addiction to an illegal substance, especially since his passion was solving crimes. 
That's my take on it, anyway.

Using Type to Get Along
by Mary Hoerr, April 19, 1999 

So what's the use of knowing type? Especially if you have trouble determining someone else's type anyway? Here's how I use it. 
First of all, although I have trouble figuring a person's whole type, I am usually able to figure out one or two functions. Then, when I communicate with that person, I take the function or functions into account. For example, if I'm working with someone I think prefers S, I will make a point to be more concrete and factual than I otherwise might be. If I'm right about the function preference, my communication with that person improves. If it doesn't improve, I pick another function and try again. 
Do you have to be "good" at the preference you're communicating with? You have to understand it well enough to use it, but you don't have to be an expert. Believe me, the people who truly have a strong preference for S will notice you're not <grin> and the same goes for the other function preferences. But most people will appreciate your effort to "speak their language". 
By the way, Dick Thompson likens communication between people with different function preferences to speaking different languages, and has developed a Communication Wheel (tm). Check out my link review of his company, High Performing Systems, Inc. at Net Links > Companies. 

How do you use your knowledge of type? Especially if you aren't adept at typing other people? Let me know by sending me your feedback. I'm always interested in your thoughts and opinions. 

Personality Type: An Owners Manual Book Review

by Mary Hoerr, April 12, 1999 

Lenore Thomson has written a book that includes the best descriptions of what the functions mean of any book I have read. I think I finally understand how F judgment works. Also a lot of fun is how she includes examples from the Star Trek universe of shows to illustrate various functions. It's amazing how well this works: Star Trek aliens can often embody pure types or missing type functions in a way that real human beings can't. 
Although the book is fun, and accessible, she can get quite complex. She describes how each type uses all four functions, T, F, S and N in each attitude. Thus, for an INTP, besides my dominant Ti, auxiliary Ne, tertiary Si and inferior Fe, she also describes how I use my "crow's nest" functions (Fi and Se) and my "double agent" functions (Te and Ni). If that's too much alphabet soup, don't worry. The in-depth descriptions of each dominant function and the Star Trek examples are quite accessible, and you can get to the rest later, if you want to. 
She also builds on Niednagel's work (see link review at Net Links > companies: The Brain Institute and book review at Features > 1999 First Quarter Features Archive: Your Key to Sports Success Book Review) on brain type, locating each dominant function in different areas of the brain. Extroverted T and F, and introverted S and N are in the "linear" left hemisphere, while extroverted S and N and introverted T and F are in the "holistic" right hemisphere. Her descriptions of the difference between extroverted F, which is linear, and introverted F, which is holistic, was fascinating, since I had always imagined F to be holistic. Also, T is not always linear - extroverted T is, but not introverted T. 
For another review of this book, you can also check out the The Enneagram and the MBTI electronic journal (see link review at Net Links > epubs).
